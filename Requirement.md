# Requirements for All Discipline Research Files

## Purpose

This document defines the quality bar and required structure for every discipline file in this repository.

The goal is not just to list majors. Each file should give a learner a **full picture of the field from undergraduate foundations to research-level maturity**, using the way strong universities actually organize teaching, methods, canonical literature, and frontier questions.

---

## Core Standard

Each discipline file should be strong enough that a serious learner can use it to answer all of the following:

1. **What does a student study at the undergraduate level?**
2. **What changes at the master's and PhD levels?**
3. **What are the main subfields and research tracks?**
4. **What methods, tools, datasets, labs, archives, or professional practices define the field?**
5. **Which textbooks are best at each stage and for each major course?**
6. **Which papers, books, essays, or landmark studies are essential for becoming an excellent researcher?**
7. **What are the most important current research frontiers and open questions?**
8. **How does the field connect to neighboring disciplines and careers?**

A file is **not complete** if it only names courses and subfields. It must also give a **credible reading roadmap** and a **research-entry map**.

---

## Required File Structure

Each discipline file should contain the following sections.

### 1. Degree Programs
Include:
- Undergraduate majors and common variants
- Master's degrees and professional master's variants where relevant
- PhD or doctoral structures
- Important professional degrees if the field uses them

### 2. Core Curriculum (Undergraduate)
Include:
- Standard lower-division and upper-division sequence
- Core required courses
- Common electives or tracks
- Brief explanation of why each course matters

### 3. Graduate Depth
Include:
- First-year graduate core where applicable
- Major specialization areas
- What becomes more theoretical, technical, empirical, archival, clinical, or studio-based at the PhD level
- Typical milestones such as quals, dissertation, lab rotations, archival training, language requirements, or practicum

### 4. Key Methods, Tools, and Research Infrastructure
Include whatever is field-appropriate:
- Mathematical and statistical methods
- Experimental methods
- Software stacks
- Lab instruments
- Datasets
- Archives
- Field sites
- Corpora
- Clinical tools
- Design studios
- Performance methods
- Regulatory or professional frameworks

### 5. Foundational Texts and Learning Resources
This section must be much more rigorous than a casual reading list.

It should include:
- **Best textbooks for each major course and level**
- Clear distinction between:
  - introductory / broad survey texts
  - standard undergraduate core texts
  - advanced undergraduate or honors texts
  - graduate core texts
  - research-level references or handbooks
- Short notes on why each recommendation is included

### 6. Foundational Literature / Canonical Reading List
Every file must include a serious literature section.

This section should contain:
- **10-50 essential works**, depending on the breadth of the field
- Grouping by purpose, such as:
  - classic / foundational
  - methods / canonical frameworks
  - landmark empirical or theoretical studies
  - modern field-defining work
  - reviews / orientation pieces
- Each entry should be specific, not vague

### 7. Open Problems and Research Frontiers
Include:
- Major unresolved questions
- Fast-moving current directions
- Important methodological or theoretical debates
- High-impact intersections with AI, computation, policy, climate, ethics, or industry where relevant

### 8. Career Paths
Include:
- Academic paths
- Industry / government / nonprofit / professional routes
- Licensure or credential structure if important
- Typical destinations rather than inflated salary lists unless salary is central to the field

### 9. Connections to Other Fields
Include:
- Closely related disciplines
- Shared methods
- Common interdisciplinary bridges

## Textbook Requirements

Textbook recommendations must be curated, not generic.

### A. Course-by-course coverage
For each major core course, list at least one strong recommendation, and where useful also list:
- the **most widely used** text
- the **most respected / highest-quality** text
- a **more accessible** text for self-study

### B. Level-aware recommendations
When applicable, distinguish among:
- Introductory / service-course text
- Standard major text
- Honors / proof-heavy / advanced text
- Graduate first-year text
- Research reference

### C. Quality bar
Recommended textbooks should satisfy at least one of these:
- Widely assigned in leading universities
- Especially influential in training researchers
- Considered a modern standard in recent years
- Exceptionally clear or high quality for self-study

### D. Notes required
Every textbook recommendation should ideally include a short note such as:
- standard undergraduate text
- common PhD core text
- classic reference but demanding
- modern and accessible
- especially strong for self-study

### E. Avoid weak recommendations
Do **not** use:
- vague statements like "standard textbook exists"
- outdated books kept only for nostalgia when clearly replaced in practice
- obscure books without a strong reason
- a single book standing in for an entire multi-course sequence

## Literature Requirements

This is the area where most files will need the most improvement.

### A. Use the right kind of literature for the field
Different disciplines have different canonical formats.

#### Paper-centered fields
Examples: physics, chemistry, biology, computer science, engineering, medicine, psychology, economics.

Requirement:
- Include **20-50 key papers**, depending on field breadth
- At least some entries should be genuine journal or conference papers, not only books
- Include both historical classics and modern breakthroughs

#### Book-centered or mixed fields
Examples: history, philosophy, literature, religious studies, classics, some social sciences.

Requirement:
- Include **10-25 essential books / monographs / essays**
- Include **5-15 major articles or chapters** where the discipline relies heavily on journal scholarship
- Do not force a paper-heavy model where it does not fit the field

#### Practice-centered professional fields
Examples: law, architecture, design, nursing, public health, management.

Requirement:
- Blend foundational books and landmark articles with:
  - policy reports
  - legal cases
  - design manifestos
  - standards documents
  - clinical guidelines
  - trial papers
  - consensus statements
  as appropriate

### B. Every entry must be concrete
Avoid placeholders such as:
- "major RCT papers"
- "important survey papers"
- "landmark papers by X"
- "key recent work"

Instead provide:
- author(s)
- year
- title
- venue or publisher when useful
- short note on why it matters

### C. Coverage expectations
The reading list should cover:
- foundational theory
- defining methods
- major empirical or interpretive breakthroughs
- at least some recent work that still shapes the active research frontier

### D. Recommended structure
A good default structure is:
1. Classic / foundational works
2. Canonical methods or framework papers
3. Landmark empirical / theoretical studies
4. Modern field-defining works
5. Reviews / synthesis / orientation

### E. Breadth vs. depth
For broad fields, it is acceptable to use a **core list plus subfield lists**.
Example:
- 12 core readings for everyone in the field
- 5-10 readings for each major specialization

That is better than one flat list that misses entire subareas.

## What “Full Picture” Means

A file should be considered to provide a full picture only if it does all of the following:

### 1. Educational completeness
It explains the progression from undergraduate survey learning to graduate specialization to research independence.

### 2. Methodological completeness
It names the actual methods researchers use, not just the topics they study.

### 3. Canonical-literature completeness
It includes a reading list that would help a strong student become research-ready.

### 4. Field-specific realism
It respects how the discipline actually works.
Examples:
- lab sciences need methods, instrumentation, and landmark papers
- humanities need historiography/theory plus monographs and canonical essays
- professional fields need practice frameworks, standards, and applied literature

### 5. Modern relevance
It includes major developments from recent decades, not only older classics.

A file that lacks any of the above should be treated as **partial**, not complete.

## Discipline-Specific Expectations

### Natural sciences
Must include:
- course sequence
- core lab or quantitative methods
- foundational textbooks
- major historical and modern papers
- key instruments, datasets, or experimental paradigms

### Engineering
Must include:
- undergraduate engineering science core
- design / modeling / simulation methods
- industrial or systems tools
- standard textbooks by subarea
- seminal technical papers and major review papers

### Computer and data fields
Must include:
- theory + systems + implementation perspective
- textbooks for core courses
- classic papers and modern conference-era milestones
- open-source tools, benchmarks, and major datasets where relevant

### Social sciences
Must include:
- theory + methods + empirical traditions
- research designs and statistical / qualitative methods
- canonical books and papers
- major identification or interpretive frameworks where relevant

### Humanities
Must include:
- canonical texts, monographs, and major essays
- historiography / theory / methods section where relevant
- language, archive, or source traditions when relevant
- modern debates and schools of interpretation

### Health and biomedical fields
Must include:
- educational path and credential structure
- core scientific or clinical curriculum
- major trial papers, guidelines, or landmark studies
- evidence standards and common research designs

### Arts, design, architecture, and performance
Must include:
- studio / practice / performance training model
- major historical works, manifestos, and criticism
- technical tools and workflows
- research or practice-based inquiry modes

### Law, policy, and business-related fields
Must include:
- foundational doctrines or frameworks
- canonical cases, articles, reports, or books
- methodological traditions
- strong practice/research split where applicable

## Editorial Standards

All files should follow these rules.

### Use precise recommendations
Prefer:
- exact titles
- real papers
- real books
- real methods
- real datasets/tools

Avoid:
- placeholders
- incomplete citations
- vague gestures toward a literature

### Keep recommendations credible
If several books are widely used, prefer the one that is:
- most assigned
- most respected
- most useful for self-study
- or clearly best for a specific track

### Add short notes, not long reviews
Each recommendation should have a short reason for inclusion.

### Balance classics and current relevance
Do not produce a reading list that ends in the 1970s.
Do not produce one that ignores classics and only lists recent trends.

### Do not force symmetry where the fields differ
A mathematics-style template should not be copied blindly into history, medicine, design, or law.
The structure should be parallel, but the literature and method sections must reflect disciplinary reality.

---

## Minimum Acceptance Checklist

A discipline file is acceptable only if all of the following are true:

- [ ] Degree levels are clearly defined
- [ ] Undergraduate curriculum is mapped in a realistic way
- [ ] Graduate depth and subfields are explained
- [ ] Methods/tools/infrastructure are described
- [ ] Textbooks are recommended course-by-course and level-by-level
- [ ] Literature list is concrete and substantial
- [ ] Reading list contains roughly **10-50 essential works**, scaled to the field
- [ ] Field-specific literature norms are respected
- [ ] Open problems and frontiers are current and meaningful
- [ ] Interdisciplinary links and career paths are included
- [ ] The file would help a serious learner become research-ready, not just informed

---

## Priority Improvements Likely Needed Across the Repository

When upgrading the discipline files, prioritize these fixes:

1. Replace vague reading-list entries with exact works.
2. Expand weak literature sections into field-appropriate **10-50 essential works**.
3. Improve textbook coverage so each core course has a clear best recommendation.
4. Distinguish undergraduate texts from graduate and research references.
5. Add recent high-impact work, not just historical classics.
6. Make methods/tools sections more concrete and discipline-specific.
7. Ensure humanities and professional fields use the right literature format instead of copying STEM conventions mechanically.

---

## Recommended Output Style for Future File Revisions

For consistency, each file should aim for:
- concise but high-information prose
- tables for curriculum and textbooks
- grouped bullet lists for literature
- short notes explaining why a recommendation matters
- explicit separation between foundational training and research frontier

The final standard should be: **a high-quality, field-aware roadmap from beginner to research-level competence**.
