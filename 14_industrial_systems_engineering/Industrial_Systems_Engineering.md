# 14. Industrial & Systems Engineering

## Degree Programs

| Level | Common programs | What changes by level |
|---|---|---|
| Undergraduate | BS in Industrial Engineering, Industrial and Systems Engineering, Operations Engineering, Manufacturing Engineering, sometimes Systems Engineering variants | Builds the quantitative, statistical, and systems-thinking foundation for improving complex operations |
| Master's | MS/MEng in Industrial Engineering, Operations Research, Systems Engineering, Supply Chain Engineering, Manufacturing, Human Factors, or Engineering Management | Adds methodological depth and sector-specific specialization |
| Doctoral | PhD in Industrial Engineering, Operations Research, Systems Engineering, Human Factors, Manufacturing Systems, or affiliated analytics programs | Centers on original research in optimization, stochastic systems, people-systems interaction, and decision-making |
| Cross-disciplinary variants | Joint programs with business, computer science, public health, statistics, transportation, policy, or defense systems | Reflects the field's unusually broad application across industries and institutions |

Industrial and systems engineering focuses on designing and improving **complex sociotechnical systems** involving people, machines, materials, information, uncertainty, and organizational constraints.

---

## 14.1 Core Curriculum (Undergraduate)

Strong ISE programs are built less around a single physical artifact than around quantitative methods for making systems work better. Compared with other engineering majors, the curriculum usually emphasizes probability, optimization, statistics, and decision-making much more heavily.

### Foundation sequence

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Calculus, linear algebra, probability, statistics** | Random variables, inference, matrix methods, optimization basics | Core mathematical language of ISE |
| **Programming and data analysis** | Python, R, MATLAB, data wrangling, visualization | Increasingly important for simulation, optimization, and analytics |
| **Engineering economics and systems thinking** | Cost models, tradeoffs, lifecycle thinking | Helps tie models to real organizational decisions |

### Core upper-division ISE areas

| Area | Typical courses | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Engineering statistics** | Probability models, inference, regression, DOE, reliability | Core empirical toolkit for process analysis and decision support |
| **Deterministic operations research** | Linear programming, integer programming, networks, dynamic programming | Fundamental optimization toolkit for planning and allocation |
| **Stochastic operations research** | Queueing, Markov chains, stochastic processes, decision analysis | Real systems face uncertainty, delays, and randomness |
| **Simulation** | Discrete-event simulation, Monte Carlo, model verification and validation | Essential when systems are too complicated for closed-form analysis |
| **Quality engineering** | SPC, control charts, process capability, Six Sigma, reliability | Central to manufacturing and service improvement |
| **Manufacturing and production systems** | Lean, facility layout, line balancing, scheduling, work design | Important for factories, fulfillment, and process operations |
| **Supply chain and logistics** | Inventory, transportation, warehousing, forecasting, network design | Major applied branch of the field |
| **Human factors / ergonomics** | Physical and cognitive ergonomics, HCI, safety, usability | Distinguishes ISE from purely mathematical OR |
| **Systems engineering** | Requirements, architecture, V-model, verification/validation, trade studies | Important for large, multi-stakeholder engineered systems |

### Typical laboratory and practical experiences

- **Simulation labs** using Arena, Simio, AnyLogic, Python, or custom tools
- **Quality and DOE labs** with control charts, capability analysis, and process-improvement cycles
- **Ergonomics / human-factors studies** with usability testing, workload measures, and task analysis
- **Operations projects** in manufacturing, healthcare, logistics, or service systems
- **Capstone design** focused on real organizations, data, and implementable improvements

## 14.2 Graduate Depth (MS & PhD)

Graduate industrial and systems engineering divides into several different research cultures: mathematical optimization and stochastic systems, manufacturing and supply chain systems, human factors and cognition, and large-scale systems engineering.

### Typical first-year graduate focus

| Area | Typical graduate emphasis | Common milestones |
|---|---|---|
| **Operations research and optimization** | Linear, integer, stochastic, dynamic, and robust optimization | Theory-heavy qualifying exams and algorithmic research |
| **Stochastic systems and analytics** | Queueing, Markov decision processes, simulation output analysis, Bayesian and statistical methods | Mathematical modeling plus computational experimentation |
| **Manufacturing and production systems** | Scheduling, digital manufacturing, process control, semiconductor or advanced manufacturing systems | Data-rich applied research and industry collaboration |
| **Supply chain and logistics** | Network design, resilience, inventory under uncertainty, transportation systems | Optimization plus empirical operations context |
| **Human factors and ergonomics** | Cognition, workload, HCI, safety, healthcare and transportation systems | Experimental design, behavioral studies, and statistical modeling |
| **Systems engineering** | Architecting large systems, MBSE, resilience, verification, sociotechnical design | Project- and systems-oriented research with defense, aerospace, or infrastructure contexts |

### Typical PhD structure

- **Advanced coursework** in optimization, stochastic processes, simulation, human factors, or systems engineering depending on track
- **Qualifying exams** that are often mathematically demanding in OR-oriented programs
- **Advisor and domain selection** in healthcare, manufacturing, logistics, transportation, energy, defense, or service operations
- **Seminars and literature reviews** that connect abstract methods to real systems and data
- **Dissertation research** focused on new methods, new models, or high-impact applications in large operational systems

### Major research tracks

#### Operations research and optimization
- Integer and combinatorial optimization, stochastic programming, robust optimization, approximation algorithms, reinforcement learning for operations, online decision-making

#### Manufacturing systems and smart production
- Scheduling, factory design, digital twins, robotics integration, semiconductor manufacturing, additive-manufacturing operations, predictive maintenance

#### Supply chain and logistics
- Inventory systems, network design, resilience, last-mile logistics, humanitarian logistics, e-commerce fulfillment, sustainability in operations

#### Human factors and ergonomics
- Human-AI teaming, HCI, transportation safety, healthcare human factors, user-centered design, workload, trust, and automation

#### Healthcare systems engineering
- Patient flow, operating-room scheduling, appointment systems, screening strategy, resource allocation, public-health logistics

#### Systems engineering and complex systems
- Requirements engineering, MBSE, architecting, system-of-systems analysis, resilience, defense and aerospace systems, infrastructure decision-making

### Core quantitative and analytical tools

| Tool family | Examples | Common role |
|---|---|---|

| **Simulation environments** | Arena, Simio, AnyLogic, SimPy | Manufacturing, logistics, healthcare, and service-system modeling |

| **Data and ML stacks** | Python, SQL, scikit-learn, PyTorch | Predictive and prescriptive analytics for operations |
| **Systems engineering tools** | SysML tools, MBSE platforms, requirements databases | Architecture, traceability, systems integration |

### Applied and experimental infrastructure

| Method / facility | Typical role |
|---|---|
| **Manufacturing and process labs** | Work design, line balancing, quality studies, digital manufacturing experiments |
| **Human-factors labs** | Usability, workload, decision support, interface testing, simulation-based studies |
| **Healthcare and operations data partnerships** | Hospital operations, clinic flow, logistics, and public-service systems research |
| **Supply chain and transportation datasets** | Forecasting, routing, disruption modeling, resilience studies |

- **IISE, INFORMS, HFES, POMS, and systems-engineering communities** depending on subfield

- **Implementation orientation**: methods are judged not only by elegance but by usability, robustness, and organizational fit

## 14.4 Foundational Texts and Learning Resources

Industrial and systems engineering requires level-aware textbook guidance because optimization, simulation, quality, ergonomics, and systems engineering each have their own canonical training path.

| Course / stage | Introductory or accessible | Standard major text | Advanced / graduate continuation | Why this set matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Engineering statistics** | Montgomery and Runger, *Applied Statistics and Probability for Engineers* | Montgomery and Runger, *Applied Statistics and Probability for Engineers* | Casella and Berger, *Statistical Inference*; Wu and Hamada, *Experiments* | Standard route from engineering statistics to deeper inference and DOE |
| **Deterministic OR / optimization** | Winston, *Operations Research: Applications and Algorithms* | Hillier and Lieberman, *Introduction to Operations Research* | Bertsimas and Tsitsiklis, *Introduction to Linear Optimization*; Nemhauser and Wolsey, *Integer and Combinatorial Optimization* | Core progression from modeling intuition to theory |
| **Stochastic processes / stochastic OR** | Ross, *Introduction to Probability Models* | Ross, *Stochastic Processes* | Puterman, *Markov Decision Processes*; Bertsekas, *Dynamic Programming and Optimal Control* | Standard path from probabilistic models to decision-making under uncertainty |
| **Queueing theory** | Short course notes and service-systems introductions | Gross, Shortle, Thompson, and Harris, *Fundamentals of Queueing Theory* | Kelly, *Reversibility and Stochastic Networks*; Whitt-style service-systems references | Canonical queueing sequence |
| **Simulation** | Banks et al., *Discrete-Event System Simulation* | Law, *Simulation Modeling and Analysis* | Nelson-style simulation experiment design texts and advanced DES literature | Standard route to serious simulation practice |
| **Quality engineering** | Montgomery, *Introduction to Statistical Quality Control* | Montgomery, *Introduction to Statistical Quality Control* | Box, Hunter, and Hunter, *Statistics for Experimenters*; Taguchi references | Standard quality-improvement and DOE progression |
| **Manufacturing systems** | Hopp and Spearman, *Factory Physics* | Hopp and Spearman, *Factory Physics* | Gershwin, *Manufacturing Systems Engineering* | Canonical variability and flow framework |
| **Supply chain and logistics** | Chopra and Meindl, *Supply Chain Management* | Simchi-Levi, Kaminsky, and Simchi-Levi, *Designing and Managing the Supply Chain* | Zipkin, *Foundations of Inventory Management*; Snyder and Shen, *Fundamentals of Supply Chain Theory* | Good path from managerial overview to analytical depth |
| **Human factors / ergonomics** | Wickens et al., *Engineering Psychology and Human Performance* | Sanders and McCormick, *Human Factors in Engineering and Design* | Salvendy, *Handbook of Human Factors and Ergonomics* | Standard route from core concepts to advanced reference |
| **Systems engineering** | Blanchard and Fabrycky, *Systems Engineering and Analysis* | Blanchard and Fabrycky, *Systems Engineering and Analysis* | INCOSE, *Systems Engineering Handbook*; Rechtin and Maier, *The Art of Systems Architecting* | Core progression from structured process to architecting complex systems |
| **Engineering economy** | Sullivan, Wicks, and Koelling, *Engineering Economy* | Sullivan, Wicks, and Koelling, *Engineering Economy* | Real-options and decision-analysis references where relevant | Essential applied decision layer for engineering systems |

### Additional learning resources

- **INFORMS and IISE tutorials and proceedings** for research norms in OR and ISE
- **Public healthcare, logistics, and transportation datasets** for applied modeling practice
- **ERP / MES / manufacturing data environments** for systems integration literacy
- **Human-factors standards and design guidelines** for usability and safety work

## 14.5 Canonical Literature and Research Reading Roadmap

Industrial and systems engineering has a mixed canon: classic operations-research papers, statistical quality foundations, ergonomics and human-factors work, and modern literature on supply chains, resilience, service systems, and AI-augmented decision-making.

### A. Foundations of operations research and optimization

1. **Dantzig, G. B. (1947), simplex-method paper.** Foundational linear-programming result.
2. **Bellman, R. (1952), dynamic-programming papers.** Core sequential-decision framework.
3. **Ford, L. R., Jr., and Fulkerson, D. R. (1956), max-flow/min-cut work.** Canonical network-optimization milestone.
4. **Johnson, S. M. (1954), optimal two- and three-stage scheduling paper in *Naval Research Logistics Quarterly*.** Classical scheduling result.
5. **Karmarkar, N. (1984), polynomial-time linear-programming paper in *Combinatorica*.** Major optimization milestone.
6. **Bertsimas, D., and Sim, M. (2004), robust discrete-optimization paper in *Operations Research*.** Important modern robust-optimization framework.

### B. Statistics, quality, and experimentation

7. **Shewhart, W. A. (1924), control-chart memorandum.** Foundation of statistical process control.
8. **Deming, W. E., quality-management works and lectures.** Major influence on industrial quality culture.
9. **Juran, J. M., quality-planning and improvement works.** Canonical quality-management orientation.
10. **Box, G. E. P., and Wilson, K. B. (1951), response-surface-methodology paper in *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society*.** Foundational DOE framework.
11. **Taguchi robust-design works.** Influential practical design framework.

### C. Inventory, queueing, and supply chains

12. **Harris, F. W. (1913), EOQ paper.** Classical inventory result.
13. **Arrow, K. J., Harris, T., and Marschak, J. (1951), inventory-theory paper.** Foundational stochastic inventory framework.
14. **Scarf, H. (1960), optimality of $(s, S)$ policies.** Canonical inventory-theory milestone.
15. **Little, J. D. C. (1961), queueing-law paper in *Operations Research*.** Classical $L = \lambda W$ relation.
16. **Clarke, G., and Wright, J. W. (1964), savings heuristic for vehicle routing in *Operations Research*.** Landmark logistics paper.
17. **Lee, H. L., Padmanabhan, V., and Whang, S. (1997), "The Bullwhip Effect in Supply Chains," *Sloan Management Review*.** Highly influential supply-chain result.
18. **Hendricks, K. B., and Singhal, V. R. (2005), disruption-performance papers.** Major resilience literature anchor.
19. **Simchi-Levi and collaborators' work on resilient and analytics-driven supply chains.** Core modern OR/logistics orientation.

### D. Manufacturing systems and production science

20. **Taylor, F. W. (1911), *The Principles of Scientific Management*.** Historically important, though not sufficient as a modern guide by itself.
21. **Hopp, W. J., and Spearman, M. L., *Factory Physics*.** Canonical manufacturing-systems framework.
22. **Gershwin, S. B., *Manufacturing Systems Engineering*.** Important advanced manufacturing reference.
23. **Modern semiconductor-fab scheduling and yield-management papers.** Key high-complexity manufacturing literature.
24. **Industry 4.0 and digital-twin manufacturing reviews.** Important current systems frontier.

### E. Human factors and sociotechnical systems

25. **Fitts, P. M. (1954), human motor-system information-capacity paper in *Journal of Experimental Psychology*.** Canonical human-factors result.
26. **Parasuraman, R., Sheridan, T. B., and Wickens, C. D. (2000), automation-levels paper in *IEEE Transactions on Systems, Man, and Cybernetics*.** Foundational human-automation allocation framework.
27. **Norman, D. A., *The Design of Everyday Things*.** Important design-oriented orientation text.
28. **Reason, J., *Human Error*.** Influential safety and human-error framework.
29. **Modern reviews on human-AI teaming, workload, trust calibration, and healthcare human factors.** Essential frontier orientation.

### F. Systems engineering and service systems

30. **Blanchard, B. S., and Fabrycky, W. J., *Systems Engineering and Analysis*.** Canonical systems-engineering entry text.
31. **Rechtin, E., and Maier, M. W., *The Art of Systems Architecting*.** Important architecting perspective.
32. **Sargent, R. G., verification-and-validation papers for simulation.** Standard simulation-methodology reference layer.
33. **Healthcare operations papers on patient flow, OR scheduling, and pandemic logistics.** Important high-impact application literature.
34. **Recent reviews on sustainable operations, circular supply chains, and carbon-aware logistics.** Important modern systems direction.

## 14.6 Open Problems and Research Frontiers

| Frontier | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **AI-augmented decision-making in operations** | A major challenge is combining optimization, simulation, prediction, and human oversight effectively |
| **Resilient and geopolitically robust supply chains** | Disruptions, climate events, and strategic dependencies have made resilience a central design problem |
| **Smart manufacturing and digital twins** | Real-time sensing and adaptive control promise better performance but require reliable models and integration |
| **Healthcare systems under chronic strain** | Capacity, scheduling, equity, and resource-allocation problems have major human impact |
| **Human-AI and human-robot teaming** | Automation only helps when trust, oversight, workload, and interfaces are well designed |
| **Sustainable operations and circular systems** | Carbon, waste, and lifecycle constraints are now core optimization objectives |
| **Large-scale service and urban systems** | Logistics, ride-sharing, mobility, emergency response, and city operations remain difficult stochastic systems |
| **Optimization with learned components** | A key frontier is deciding when ML-based prediction improves or destabilizes downstream decisions |

### Active current directions

- **Reinforcement learning and approximate dynamic programming for operations**
- **Prescriptive analytics and decision-focused machine learning**
- **Digital-thread and cyber-physical manufacturing systems**
- **Resilience metrics for supply chains and infrastructure systems**
- **Human factors for autonomous vehicles, aviation, and clinical systems**
- **Carbon-aware logistics and circular-economy optimization**
- **Health-system simulation and equity-aware resource allocation**

Industrial and systems engineering careers are unusually flexible because the field trains people to improve **how complex systems make decisions and deliver performance under uncertainty**.

## 14.9 What a Full Picture of Industrial and Systems Engineering Research Requires

A research-ready industrial and systems engineering roadmap should make clear:

1. how students move from **probability, statistics, optimization, and simulation** into manufacturing, supply chains, quality, human factors, and systems engineering;
2. how graduate training separates into different research cultures such as **OR theory, stochastic systems, human factors, healthcare systems, and manufacturing analytics**;
3. which **methods and infrastructures** matter most, including solvers, simulation environments, data systems, human-factors labs, and real organizational partnerships;
4. which **textbooks** anchor each major course from OR and queueing through quality, simulation, ergonomics, and systems engineering;
5. which **papers and framework works** define simplex, dynamic programming, EOQ, control charts, Little's Law, bullwhip effects, automation theory, and resilient operations;
6. and how the field ultimately depends on **implementation in real systems**: uncertainty, incentives, usability, data quality, and organizational constraints.

That is what turns industrial and systems engineering from a bag of optimization tools into a real roadmap toward research capability.
