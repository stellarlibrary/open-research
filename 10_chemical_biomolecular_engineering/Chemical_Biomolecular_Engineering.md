# 10. Chemical & Biomolecular Engineering

## Degree Programs

| Level | Common programs | What changes by level |
|---|---|---|
| Undergraduate | Chemical Engineering, Biomolecular Engineering, Biochemical Engineering variants | Builds transport, thermodynamics, reaction engineering, process design, and scale-up thinking |
| Master's | MS/MEng in Chemical Engineering, Biochemical Engineering, Polymer Engineering, Process Systems Engineering | Adds specialization, advanced modeling, and industry- or thesis-driven depth |
| Doctoral | PhD in Chemical Engineering, often with tracks in catalysis, materials, bioengineering, energy, or systems | Focuses on original research, pilot-scale reasoning, and dissertation work |

Chemical engineering is the discipline of taking chemistry, biology, and materials concepts and making them work **at scale, under constraints, and with economic and safety realism**.

---

## 10.1 Core Curriculum (Undergraduate)

Strong chemical engineering programs train students to think in balances, constitutive laws, transport, and process integration rather than in individual reactions alone.

### Foundation sequence

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Calculus, differential equations, linear algebra** | Core mathematical modeling tools | Needed for transport, control, and reactor analysis |
| **Physics and chemistry** | General chemistry, organic chemistry, physical chemistry, physics | Supplies the molecular and thermodynamic basis of the field |
| **Programming and numerical methods** | MATLAB, Python, optimization, data analysis | Important for simulation, control, and process systems work |
| **Material and energy balances** | Mass balances, stoichiometry, recycle, combustion, phase accounting | The foundational systems-thinking course in ChemE |

### Core upper-division areas

| Area | Typical courses | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Thermodynamics** | EOS, fugacity, activity, phase equilibria, solution thermodynamics | Connects molecular behavior to separations and design |
| **Transport phenomena** | Momentum, heat, and mass transport | Core analytical framework of the discipline |
| **Fluid mechanics and heat transfer** | Pipe flow, pumps, exchangers, convection, boiling/condensation | Essential for plant and process equipment |
| **Mass transfer and separations** | Diffusion, distillation, absorption, extraction, membranes, adsorption | Separations often dominate process cost and complexity |
| **Chemical reaction engineering** | Kinetics, reactor design, catalysis, RTD, nonideal reactors | Core to reaction scale-up and process synthesis |
| **Process dynamics and control** | Transfer functions, PID, feedback, stability, MPC introduction | Required for safe and stable operation |
| **Process design / capstone** | PFDs, P&IDs, economics, safety, environmental analysis, HAZOP | Integrates engineering science into complete process systems |

### Typical practical and laboratory experiences

- **Unit-operations labs**: pumps, heat exchangers, distillation columns, absorbers, fluidized beds
- **Reaction-engineering labs**: kinetic parameter estimation, CSTR/PFR behavior, catalyst testing
- **Process control labs**: sensors, actuators, tuning, disturbance rejection
- **Bioprocess labs** where biomolecular tracks exist: fermentation, chromatography, filtration, cell culture basics
- **Capstone design** with process simulation, economics, safety, and sustainability tradeoffs

## 10.2 Graduate Depth (MS & PhD)

Graduate chemical engineering shifts from using standard process models to developing new catalysts, materials, bioprocesses, control strategies, and multiscale models. The field is unusually strong at connecting **fundamental transport and thermodynamics to industrially relevant systems**.

### Typical first-year graduate focus

| Area | Typical graduate emphasis | Common milestones |
|---|---|---|
| **Catalysis and reaction engineering** | Surface kinetics, catalytic cycles, reactor modeling, electrocatalysis, photocatalysis | Proposal exam, kinetics/modeling depth |
| **Transport and complex fluids** | Multiphase flow, interfacial transport, rheology, porous media, microfluidics | Theory-heavy qualifying exams |
| **Biochemical / biomolecular engineering** | Bioreactors, metabolic engineering, downstream processing, cell therapies, synthetic biology platforms | Lab-intensive and data-rich research |
| **Materials / polymers** | Polymer physics, self-assembly, membranes, soft matter, energy materials | Coupled synthesis and characterization workflows |
| **Process systems engineering** | Optimization, uncertainty, process control, digital twins, supply chain models | Strong computational and mathematical focus |
| **Electrochemical engineering / energy** | Batteries, fuel cells, electrolyzers, corrosion, transport in porous electrodes | Multiphysics modeling plus experiments |

### Typical PhD structure

- **Advanced coursework and qualifying exams** in transport, thermodynamics, and the chosen research area
- **Advisor and project selection** in catalysis, bioengineering, systems, materials, or energy labs
- **Simulation, pilot-scale, and/or experimental platform training**
- **Seminars and literature reviews** with strong emphasis on mechanistic interpretation
- **Publication-driven dissertation** connecting first-principles reasoning to scalable processes

### Major research tracks

#### Catalysis and reaction engineering
- Heterogeneous catalysis, electrocatalysis, photocatalysis, microreactors, reactor intensification, CO2 conversion, ammonia, selective oxidation, catalytic materials

#### Transport phenomena and multiphase systems
- Interfacial transport, porous media, complex fluids, granular flows, colloids, microfluidics, membrane transport, heat and mass transfer under strong coupling

#### Biochemical and biomolecular engineering
- Fermentation, cell culture, metabolic engineering, downstream purification, biologics, mRNA/LNP manufacturing, gene and cell therapy production, biosystems design

#### Polymers, soft matter, and materials engineering
- Controlled polymerization, rheology, self-assembly, membranes, nanocomposites, sustainable polymers, functional materials, biomaterials

#### Process systems engineering
- Optimization, process synthesis, control, scheduling, uncertainty quantification, digital twins, process analytics, AI for operations

#### Electrochemical and energy engineering
- Batteries, fuel cells, electrolyzers, carbon capture, separations for energy systems, hydrogen, electrified chemical manufacturing

### Core analytical and simulation tools

| Tool family | Examples | Common role |
|---|---|---|
| **Process simulation** | Aspen Plus, Aspen HYSYS, gPROMS, DWSIM | Flowsheets, thermodynamics, design studies, optimization |
| **Mathematical and data tools** | MATLAB, Python, Julia | Modeling, control, optimization, ML, data analysis |

| **Electrochemical and battery modeling** | COMSOL, Newman-model implementations, PyBaMM | Cells, electrodes, transport, degradation |
| **Molecular and materials tools** | Gaussian, ORCA, VASP, LAMMPS, GROMACS | Catalysis, polymers, surfaces, molecular design |

### Experimental and unit-operations infrastructure

| Method / facility | Typical role |
|---|---|
| **Pilot and unit-operations labs** | Distillation, absorption, heat exchange, filtration, fluid flow |
| **Reaction systems** | Batch, CSTR, PFR, packed-bed, microreactor, electrochemical cells |
| **Analytical chemistry platforms** | GC, HPLC, MS, NMR, spectroscopy, rheometry, particle sizing |

- **Safety and process-risk culture**: HAZOP, inherently safer design, hazard analysis, relief design, environmental compliance
- **Strong industry interface** in chemicals, pharma, semiconductors, energy, and consumer products

## 10.4 Foundational Texts and Learning Resources

Chemical engineering needs course-by-course, level-aware guidance because undergraduate system-building texts, graduate theory texts, and industrial references each play different roles.

| Course / stage | Introductory or accessible | Standard major text | Advanced / graduate continuation | Why this set matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Material and energy balances** | Felder, Rousseau, and Bullard, *Elementary Principles of Chemical Processes* | Felder, Rousseau, and Bullard, *Elementary Principles of Chemical Processes* | Reklaitis problem-oriented references | The canonical first ChemE course text |
| **Thermodynamics** | Elliott and Lira, *Introductory Chemical Engineering Thermodynamics* | Smith, Van Ness, Abbott, and Swihart, *Introduction to Chemical Engineering Thermodynamics* | Prausnitz et al., *Molecular Thermodynamics of Fluid-Phase Equilibria* | Standard route from undergraduate thermo to research-level phase behavior |
| **Fluid mechanics / heat and mass transfer** | Çengel and Ghajar, *Heat and Mass Transfer* | Geankoplis, *Transport Processes and Separation Process Principles* | Bird, Stewart, and Lightfoot, *Transport Phenomena* | Core progression into the central analytical framework of ChemE |
| **Separations** | Geankoplis, *Transport Processes and Separation Process Principles* | Seader, Henley, and Roper, *Separation Process Principles* | Wankat, *Separation Process Engineering* | Standard route from integrated transport to dedicated separations |
| **Reaction engineering** | Fogler, *Essentials of Chemical Reaction Engineering* | Fogler, *Elements of Chemical Reaction Engineering* | Froment, Bischoff, and De Wilde, *Chemical Reactor Analysis and Design* | Fogler is the standard undergraduate text |
| **Process control** | Bequette, *Process Control: Modeling, Design, and Simulation* | Seborg, Edgar, Mellichamp, and Doyle, *Process Dynamics and Control* | Rawlings, Mayne, and Diehl, *Model Predictive Control* | Standard progression from PID to advanced control |
| **Process design** | Turton et al., *Analysis, Synthesis, and Design of Chemical Processes* | Turton et al., *Analysis, Synthesis, and Design of Chemical Processes* | Towler and Sinnott, *Chemical Engineering Design* | Core capstone and plant-design pathway |
| **Biochemical engineering** | Shuler and Kargi, *Bioprocess Engineering* | Bailey and Ollis, *Biochemical Engineering Fundamentals* | Nielsen and Villadsen-style systems-biology/bioprocess texts | Standard route for bioprocess specialization |
| **Electrochemical engineering** | — | Newman and Thomas-Alyea, *Electrochemical Systems* | Bockris and Reddy; modern battery texts | Canonical electrochemical engineering reference |
| **Polymer engineering / science** | Young and Lovell, *Introduction to Polymers* | Odian, *Principles of Polymerization* | Flory, *Principles of Polymer Chemistry* | Standard polymer track progression |
| **Optimization / process systems** | — | Edgar, Himmelblau, and Lasdon, *Optimization of Chemical Processes* | Biegler, *Nonlinear Programming* | Important for systems-oriented ChemE |

### Additional learning resources

- **AIChE concept warehouse and process-safety resources**
- **Aspen tutorials and unit-operations labs** for practical flowsheet literacy
- **NIST and materials/property databases** for engineering design work
- **Bioprocess and GMP training resources** for biomolecular-manufacturing pathways

## 10.5 Canonical Literature and Research Reading Roadmap

Chemical engineering is a mixed field: foundational papers in transport, adsorption, reactor theory, and process systems matter alongside influential books and modern application-driven papers in catalysis, energy, bioprocessing, and sustainability.

### A. Foundations of transport, reactors, and separation

1. **Langmuir, I. (1918), adsorption-isotherm papers in *Journal of the American Chemical Society*.** Canonical adsorption framework.
2. **Brunauer, Emmett, and Teller (1938), BET adsorption paper in *JACS*.** Fundamental surface-area and adsorption model.
3. **Ergun, S. (1952), packed-bed pressure-drop paper in *Chemical Engineering Progress*.** Standard packed-bed relation.
4. **Danckwerts, P. V. (1953), residence-time-distribution and flow-system papers in *Chemical Engineering Science*.** Core reactor nonideality framework.
5. **Taylor, G. I. (1953) and Aris, R. (1956), dispersion papers.** Foundational mass-transport framework for laminar flow and reactors.
6. **Hougen-Watson catalytic-kinetics framework papers.** Central to heterogeneous reaction engineering.

### B. Process control, systems, and optimization

7. **Chilton and Colburn transfer-analogy papers.** Important unifying transport concept.
8. **Cutler, C. R., and Ramaker, B. L. (1979), dynamic matrix control papers.** Major process-control milestone.
9. **Grossmann, I. E., and Sargent-era process-systems-engineering papers.** Foundational optimization and synthesis framework.
10. **Biegler and collaborators' nonlinear-programming and dynamic optimization papers.** Key modern systems references.

### C. Biochemical and biomolecular engineering

11. **Michaelis, L., and Menten, M. L. (1913), enzyme-kinetics paper.** Fundamental to bioreaction engineering.
12. **Bailey, J. E. (1991), "Toward a Science of Metabolic Engineering," *Science*.** Landmark statement of metabolic engineering.
13. **Stephanopoulos and Vallino metabolic-flux-analysis papers.** Crucial systems-biology and bioprocess bridge.
14. **Keasling-group artemisinin-biosynthesis papers in *Nature* and *Nature Biotechnology*.** Iconic metabolic-engineering success story.
15. **Modern mRNA/LNP and cell-therapy manufacturing reviews.** Essential for current biomolecular engineering practice.

### D. Energy, electrochemistry, and sustainable processes

16. **Doyle, M., Fuller, T. F., and Newman, J. (1993), lithium-ion model paper in *Journal of the Electrochemical Society*.** Canonical battery-engineering model.
17. **Tarascon, J.-M., and Armand, M. (2001), battery challenges paper in *Nature*.** Core energy-storage perspective.
18. **Nørskov and collaborators' electrocatalysis descriptor and volcano-plot papers.** Major catalysis-energy framework.
19. **Keith, D. W., Holmes, G., St. Angelo, D., and Heidel, K. (2018), direct-air-capture process paper in *Joule*.** Important current carbon-capture milestone.
20. **Modern membrane-separation reviews by Sholl, Lively, and others.** Central to separations and energy efficiency.

### E. Polymers, sustainability, and current frontier

21. **Carothers, W. H. (1936), polymerization fundamentals.** Foundational polymer engineering history.
22. **Anastas and Warner's green-chemistry framework works.** Key sustainability orientation.
23. **Recent circular-plastics, chemical-recycling, and process-intensification reviews.** Important for current industrial and environmental directions.
24. **Digital-twin and autonomous-process-control reviews.** Useful orientation to AI-enabled process engineering.

## 10.6 Open Problems and Research Frontiers

| Frontier | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Decarbonized chemical manufacturing** | Major challenge for energy, emissions, and industrial competitiveness |
| **Carbon capture, utilization, and storage** | Core process-engineering problem with thermodynamic and economic constraints |
| **Green hydrogen and electrified synthesis** | Central to the energy transition and ammonia/fuel pathways |
| **Biomanufacturing at industrial scale** | Key for biologics, mRNA, cell therapies, and sustainable chemicals |
| **Circular plastics and sustainable polymers** | Important for waste, carbon, and materials sustainability |
| **Digital twins and autonomous process control** | Connects sensing, optimization, simulation, and operations |
| **Advanced batteries, fuel cells, and electrolyzers** | Requires strong electrochemical and transport engineering |
| **Membrane and intensified separations** | High leverage because separations dominate energy use in many plants |

### Active current directions

- **Electrochemical CO2 conversion and ammonia synthesis**
- **Process electrification and modular chemical plants**
- **Continuous bioprocessing and advanced downstream purification**
- **AI-guided process optimization and predictive maintenance**
- **High-performance membranes and adsorption materials**
- **Sustainable polymer chemistry and plastic upcycling**
- **Battery manufacturing and electrode process engineering**

## 10.9 What a Full Picture of Chemical and Biomolecular Engineering Research Requires

A research-ready chemical engineering roadmap should make clear:

1. how students move from **balances and thermodynamics** into transport, separations, reactions, control, and design;
2. how the field depends on **scale-up, safety, and economics** as much as on chemistry or biology itself;
3. how graduate training shifts toward **catalysis, bioengineering, systems, electrochemistry, and advanced materials**;
4. which **textbooks** anchor the core course sequence and main advanced tracks;
5. which **papers and framework works** define adsorption, transport, RTD, metabolic engineering, batteries, and process systems;
6. and how the field connects to **energy, manufacturing, medicine, sustainability, and materials**.

That is what turns a chemical engineering overview into a genuine roadmap toward research capability.
