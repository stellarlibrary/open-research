# 5. Earth & Environmental Sciences

## Degree Programs

| Level | Common programs | What changes by level |
|---|---|---|
| Undergraduate | Geology, Earth Science, Environmental Science, Geophysics, Atmospheric Science, Oceanography, Hydrology, Planetary Science | Builds field observation, laboratory analysis, and quantitative Earth-system reasoning |
| Master's | MS in Geology, Geophysics, Geochemistry, Environmental Science, Atmospheric Science, Oceanography, Hydrology, GIS/Remote Sensing | Adds technical specialization, more independent field or modeling work, and thesis-level synthesis |
| Doctoral | PhD in Geology, Geophysics, Geochemistry, Paleontology, Atmospheric Science, Oceanography, Climate Science, Hydrology, Planetary Science | Centered on original research, field campaigns, instrumentation, large datasets, and dissertation work |

Earth and environmental sciences are often organized as a single Earth-system enterprise even when departments are split among geology, atmosphere/ocean, climate, and environmental tracks.

## 5.1 Core Curriculum (Undergraduate)

Strong Earth science training teaches students to reason across **deep time, large spatial scales, and coupled physical systems**. The discipline is distinctive because field observation, mapping, and data integration matter as much as classroom theory.

### Lower division (years 1-2)

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical Geology** | Minerals, rocks, tectonics, earthquakes, volcanism, geomorphology, geologic time | Introduces the materials and processes of the solid Earth |
| **Historical Geology / Earth History** | Stratigraphy, fossils, environmental change, mass extinctions, deep-time chronology | Trains students to think historically across billions of years |
| **Earth System Science** | Coupled atmosphere, hydrosphere, cryosphere, geosphere, biosphere, carbon and water cycles | Provides the modern integrative framework |
| **General Chemistry** | Bonding, equilibrium, thermodynamics, solution chemistry | Needed for mineralogy, geochemistry, aqueous systems, and atmospheric chemistry |
| **Calculus and Physics** | Mechanics, waves, fluids, thermodynamics, electromagnetism | Supports geophysics, climate dynamics, hydrology, and modeling |
| **Introductory Biology** | Ecology, evolution, organismal biology | Important for paleontology, biogeochemistry, and environmental science |

### Upper division core areas

| Area | Typical courses | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Solid Earth** | Mineralogy, Petrology, Structural Geology, Sedimentology, Stratigraphy | Builds literacy in rocks, minerals, deformation, and Earth history |
| **Geophysics and Geodynamics** | Seismology, Gravity and Magnetics, Geodynamics, Plate Tectonics | Quantifies Earth structure and tectonic processes |
| **Geochemistry** | Thermodynamics, isotopes, aqueous geochemistry, biogeochemistry | Connects chemistry to rocks, fluids, climate, and planetary evolution |
| **Atmosphere, Ocean, and Climate** | Meteorology, Oceanography, Climate Science, Atmospheric Chemistry | Covers circulation, radiation, feedbacks, and Earth-system coupling |
| **Hydrology and Environmental Systems** | Hydrology, Hydrogeology, Environmental Geology, Soil and Water Systems | Links Earth science to water, contamination, hazards, and management |
| **GIS, Remote Sensing, and Data Methods** | Spatial analysis, satellite data, LiDAR, programming, numerical modeling | Essential for modern Earth observation and applied geoscience |

### Defining practical experiences

- **Field trips and field methods courses**: outcrop interpretation, mapping, sampling, notebook practice
- **Field camp** in many geology programs: mapping, structural measurements, stratigraphic section work
- **Laboratory training**: petrographic microscopy, geochemical analysis, sediment analysis, isotope methods
- **Remote sensing and GIS labs**: satellite imagery, DEMs, change detection, hazard and resource mapping
- **Research cruises or atmospheric observations** in oceanography and meteorology tracks

## 5.2 Graduate Depth (MS & PhD)

Graduate Earth science requires moving from descriptive competence to **model-based, instrument-intensive, and inference-heavy research**. Students usually specialize sharply while staying conversant with Earth-system coupling.

### Typical first-year graduate focus

| Area | Typical graduate emphasis | Common milestones |
|---|---|---|
| **Geology / tectonics** | Advanced structural analysis, thermochronology, basin evolution, magma and metamorphic processes | Proposal, field campaign design, thin-section and analytical workflow mastery |
| **Geophysics** | Seismic theory, inverse methods, geodesy, computational modeling | Quantitative exams, coding-intensive research |
| **Geochemistry** | Radiogenic and stable isotopes, trace elements, thermodynamic modeling, reactive transport | Lab methods plus instrument-facility training |
| **Atmosphere / climate / ocean** | Fluid dynamics, radiative transfer, climate dynamics, Earth-system models | Model evaluation, reanalysis work, field or observational datasets |
| **Hydrology / environmental geoscience** | Groundwater modeling, contaminant transport, watershed analysis, critical-zone science | Site-based research and applied modeling |
| **Planetary science** | Planetary surfaces, atmospheres, geophysics, remote sensing, mission data analysis | Mission-data literacy and comparative planetology |

### Typical PhD training structure

- **Advisor selection and dissertation topic definition**
- **Qualifying or candidacy exams** in quantitative and subfield-specific material
- **Field seasons, cruises, observatory work, or major computational campaigns**
- **Facility training** for isotope labs, microprobes, mass spectrometers, seismic arrays, or supercomputing systems
- **Conference participation** through AGU, GSA, AMS, EGU, MRS, or planetary-science meetings
- **Publication and dissertation** built around datasets, models, or integrated Earth-system interpretation

### Major research tracks

#### Geology and tectonics
- Plate tectonics, mountain building, deformation, volcanology, sedimentary basins, metamorphism, crust-mantle interactions

#### Geophysics
- Seismology, geodesy, geomagnetism, exploration geophysics, mantle convection, earthquake mechanics, planetary interiors

#### Geochemistry and cosmochemistry
- Isotope geochemistry, geochronology, aqueous geochemistry, biogeochemistry, meteorites, mantle evolution, carbon cycle

#### Paleontology and Earth history
- Mass extinctions, paleoecology, paleoclimate proxies, evolutionary history, astrobiology, fossil systems

#### Atmosphere, ocean, and climate science
- Weather dynamics, aerosols, climate feedbacks, circulation, ocean heat transport, cryosphere, extreme-event attribution

#### Hydrology and environmental geoscience
- Surface water, groundwater, water quality, reactive transport, contaminant remediation, critical zone, water-resource systems

#### Planetary science
- Mars, icy moons, planetary atmospheres, planetary surfaces, exoplanet habitability, small bodies, mission science

## 5.3 Key Methods, Tools, and Research Infrastructure

### Field and observational methods

| Method | Typical role |
|---|---|
| **Geologic mapping and stratigraphic logging** | Reconstructing structures, depositional history, and basin evolution |
| **Sampling of rocks, sediments, soils, water, gases, and ice** | Connecting field observations to lab analysis |
| **GPS / GNSS, InSAR, LiDAR, drone surveys** | Measuring deformation, topography, hazards, and landscape change |
| **Weather stations, buoys, radiosondes, ocean profilers** | Atmospheric and ocean observations |
| **Seismic arrays and passive-source monitoring** | Earthquake and Earth-structure analysis |
| **Field ecology and environmental monitoring** | Coupled environmental and Earth-system measurements |

### Laboratory and analytical infrastructure

| Method | Typical role |
|---|---|
| **Petrographic microscopy** | Mineral and rock texture analysis |
| **XRD / XRF / SEM-EDS / EPMA** | Mineral identification and composition |
| **ICP-MS / ICP-OES / TIMS / SIMS** | Trace elements, isotopes, geochronology, cosmochemistry |
| **Stable isotope mass spectrometry** | Paleoclimate, hydrology, carbon and nutrient cycling |
| **Radiometric and luminescence dating** | Chronology for rocks, sediments, and archaeological-environmental materials |
| **Core logging and sediment analysis** | Stratigraphy, paleoenvironmental reconstruction, paleoclimate archives |

### Computational and modeling tools

| Tool family | Examples | Common role |
|---|---|---|
| **Numerical Earth-system modeling** | CESM, GFDL, WRF, ROMS | Climate, weather, ocean, and coupled-system simulation |
| **Hydrologic and subsurface modeling** | MODFLOW, HYDRUS, PFLOTRAN | Groundwater, vadose-zone, and contaminant transport |
| **Seismology and geophysics software** | ObsPy, SPECFEM, GMT, GPlates | Seismic analysis, wave simulation, tectonic reconstruction |
| **GIS and remote sensing** | ArcGIS, QGIS, Google Earth Engine | Spatial analysis and satellite-data workflows |
| **Scientific programming** | Python, MATLAB, R, Julia | Data processing, inversion, visualization, and reproducible analysis |

### Professional infrastructure

- **Field stations, marine labs, observatories, drilling programs, and research vessels**
- **Satellite and mission data archives** from NASA, NOAA, ESA, IRIS/FDSN, Copernicus, EarthChem, PANGAEA
- **Large shared instruments**: microprobes, clean isotope labs, mass spectrometers, supercomputing clusters
- **Core professional societies**: AGU, GSA, AMS, EGU, ASLO, Geological Society of London

## 5.4 Foundational Texts and Learning Resources

Earth and environmental sciences need course-by-course guidance because different tracks rely on different mixtures of fieldwork, chemistry, physics, and modeling.

| Course / stage | Introductory or accessible | Standard major text | Advanced / graduate continuation | Why this set matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Introductory Geology / Earth Science** | Marshak, *Earth: Portrait of a Planet* | Grotzinger and Jordan et al., *Understanding Earth* | Press and Siever et al., *Understanding Earth* variants | Grotzinger/Jordan is the common research-university foundation |
| **Mineralogy** | Nesse, *Introduction to Mineralogy* | Klein and Dutrow, *Manual of Mineral Science* | Deer, Howie, and Zussman, *An Introduction to the Rock-Forming Minerals* | Builds crystal and mineral literacy essential for geology |
| **Igneous and Metamorphic Petrology** | Philpotts and Ague, *Principles of Igneous and Metamorphic Petrology* | Winter, *Principles of Igneous and Metamorphic Petrology* | Best, *Igneous and Metamorphic Petrology*; phase-equilibria references | Winter is a standard upper-division and graduate bridge |
| **Sedimentology / Stratigraphy** | Nichols, *Sedimentology and Stratigraphy* | Boggs, *Principles of Sedimentology and Stratigraphy* | Catuneanu, *Principles of Sequence Stratigraphy* | Core for basin analysis and Earth-history interpretation |
| **Structural Geology / Tectonics** | Marshak and Mitra, *Basic Methods of Structural Geology* | Twiss and Moores, *Structural Geology* | Fossen, *Structural Geology* | Supports map interpretation and tectonic reasoning |
| **Geophysics / Geodynamics** | Fowler, *The Solid Earth* | Turcotte and Schubert, *Geodynamics* | Aki and Richards, *Quantitative Seismology* | Gives the quantitative physics of the Earth interior |
| **Geochemistry** | Rollinson, *Using Geochemical Data* | White, *Geochemistry* | Faure and Mensing, *Isotopes*; Albarède, *Geochemistry* | Essential for isotope, crust-mantle, and environmental applications |
| **Atmospheric Science** | Ahrens, *Meteorology Today* | Wallace and Hobbs, *Atmospheric Science: An Introductory Survey* | Holton and Hakim, *An Introduction to Dynamic Meteorology* | Standard pathway from survey to graduate dynamics |
| **Climate Science** | Archer, *Global Warming: Understanding the Forecast* | Hartmann, *Global Physical Climatology* | Pierrehumbert, *Principles of Planetary Climate* | Strong progression from accessible to physically rigorous |
| **Oceanography** | Trujillo and Thurman, *Essentials of Oceanography* | Talley, Pickard, Emery, and Swift, *Descriptive Physical Oceanography* | Vallis, *Atmospheric and Oceanic Fluid Dynamics* | Covers the observational-to-dynamical transition |
| **Hydrology / Hydrogeology** | Dingman, *Physical Hydrology* | Fetter, *Applied Hydrogeology* | Freeze and Cherry, *Groundwater* | Standard route into water science and subsurface flow |
| **GIS / Remote Sensing** | Bolstad, *GIS Fundamentals* | Longley et al., *Geographic Information Systems and Science* | Campbell and Wynne, *Introduction to Remote Sensing* | Important because spatial analysis is now core infrastructure |
| **Planetary Science** | Bennett et al., *The Cosmic Perspective* planetary chapters | de Pater and Lissauer, *Planetary Sciences* | Pierrehumbert and mission literature | Standard entry into comparative planetology |

### Additional learning resources

- **USGS, NOAA, NASA Earthdata, Copernicus, EarthChem, PANGAEA** for live datasets
- **AGU and GSA short courses** for methods, coding, and field interpretation
- **OneGeology, Macrostrat, and geological map services** for mapping literacy

## 5.5 Canonical Literature and Research Reading Roadmap

Earth science is strongly paper-centered, but classic books and synthesis reports also matter. A research-ready reader should know the foundational frameworks that changed how we understand the planet.

### A. Foundations of climate, tectonics, and Earth-system thinking

1. **Arrhenius, S. (1896), "On the Influence of Carbonic Acid in the Air upon the Temperature of the Ground."** Early greenhouse-effect calculation.
2. **Wegener, A. (1912), "Die Entstehung der Kontinente," and related continental-drift works.** Famous pre-plate-tectonics mobilist argument.
3. **Hess, H. H. (1962), "History of Ocean Basins," in *Petrologic Studies*.** Crucial seafloor-spreading paper.
4. **Vine, F. J., and Matthews, D. H. (1963), "Magnetic Anomalies over Oceanic Ridges," *Nature*.** Key evidence for seafloor spreading.
5. **Wilson, J. T. (1965), "A New Class of Faults and Their Bearing on Continental Drift," *Nature*.** Introduced transform faults into tectonic theory.
6. **Morgan, W. J. (1968), "Rises, Trenches, Great Faults, and Crustal Blocks," *Journal of Geophysical Research*.** Plate-motion and hotspot framework.
7. **Keeling, C. D. (1960), "The Concentration and Isotopic Abundances of Carbon Dioxide in Rural Areas," *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*.** Foundation of the Keeling-curve era.
8. **Lorenz, E. N. (1963), "Deterministic Nonperiodic Flow," *Journal of the Atmospheric Sciences*.** Chaos and weather predictability.

### B. Geophysics, seismology, and quantitative Earth processes

9. **McKenzie, D. P., and Parker, R. L. (1967), "The North Pacific: An Example of Tectonics on a Sphere," *Nature*.** Plate kinematics as geometry and mechanics.
10. **Kanamori, H. (1977), "The Energy Release in Great Earthquakes," *Journal of Geophysical Research*.** Important foundation for moment magnitude and earthquake scaling.
11. **Aki, K., and Richards, P. G. related seismology work culminating in *Quantitative Seismology*.** Canonical theoretical framework for seismic waves.
12. **Tarantola, A. (1987), *Inverse Problem Theory*.** Foundational for modern geophysical inversion.

### C. Paleoclimate, carbon cycle, and climate change

13. **Milankovitch, M. (1941), *Canon of Insolation and the Ice-Age Problem*.** Orbital-forcing framework.
14. **Hays, J. D., Imbrie, J., and Shackleton, N. J. (1976), "Variations in the Earth's Orbit: Pacemaker of the Ice Ages," *Science*.** Landmark empirical validation of orbital forcing.
15. **Manabe, S., and Wetherald, R. T. (1967), "Thermal Equilibrium of the Atmosphere with a Given Distribution of Relative Humidity," *Journal of the Atmospheric Sciences*.** Key climate-modeling paper.
16. **Charney, J. et al. (1979), *Carbon Dioxide and Climate: A Scientific Assessment*.** Foundational climate-sensitivity synthesis.
17. **Broecker, W. S. (1991), "The Great Ocean Conveyor," *Oceanography*.** Iconic thermohaline-circulation concept paper.
18. **Petit, J. R. et al. (1999), "Climate and Atmospheric History of the Past 420,000 Years from the Vostok Ice Core," *Nature*.** Landmark paleoclimate record.
19. **IPCC assessment physical-science chapters, especially AR5 and AR6.** Essential synthesis literature for current climate science.

### D. Earth history, extinction, and planetary limits

20. **Alvarez, L. W. et al. (1980), "Extraterrestrial Cause for the Cretaceous-Tertiary Extinction," *Science*.** Transformed extinction research.
21. **Raup, D. M., and Sepkoski, J. J. (1982), "Mass Extinctions in the Marine Fossil Record," *Science*.** Central paleobiological analysis.
22. **Rockström, J. et al. (2009), "A Safe Operating Space for Humanity," *Nature*.** Planetary-boundaries framework.
23. **Steffen, W. et al. (2015), "Planetary Boundaries: Guiding Human Development on a Changing Planet," *Science*.** Updated planetary-boundaries synthesis.

### E. Atmospheric, environmental, and modern predictive directions

24. **Oreskes, N. (2004), "The Scientific Consensus on Climate Change," *Science*.** Influential science-policy synthesis.
25. **Meinshausen, M. et al. (2009), "Greenhouse-Gas Emission Targets for Limiting Global Warming to 2°C," *Nature*.** Important carbon-budget framing.
26. **Hansen, J. et al. (1988), "Global Climate Changes as Forecast by Goddard Institute for Space Studies Three-Dimensional Model," *Journal of Geophysical Research*.** Early influential climate-forecasting paper.
27. **Lam, R. et al. (2023), "GraphCast: Learning Skillful Medium-Range Global Weather Forecasting," *Science*.** Major AI-weather milestone.
28. **Bi, K. et al. (2023), "Accurate Medium-Range Global Weather Forecasting with 3D Neural Networks," *Nature*.** Pangu-Weather and the modern ML forecasting turn.

### F. Orientation and synthesis works

29. **Pierrehumbert, R. T. (2010), *Principles of Planetary Climate*.** Core climate-physics reference.
30. **Turcotte, D. L., and Schubert, G. (2014 ed.), *Geodynamics*.** Canonical synthesis for quantitative Earth processes.
31. **Wallace, J. M., and Hobbs, P. V. (2006 ed.), *Atmospheric Science*.** Standard orientation to atmospheric research.
32. **de Pater, I., and Lissauer, J. J. (2015 ed.), *Planetary Sciences*.** Strong comparative-planetology orientation.

A strong next step is to add **subfield reading ladders** for seismology, isotope geochemistry, paleoclimate, ocean circulation, hydrology, and planetary exploration.

## 5.6 Open Problems and Research Frontiers

| Frontier | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Climate sensitivity and cloud feedbacks** | Central uncertainty in long-term warming projections |
| **Origin and evolution of plate tectonics** | Fundamental to Earth's uniqueness and long-term habitability |
| **Earthquake nucleation and hazard forecasting** | Important for risk reduction but still poorly resolved mechanistically |
| **Deep Earth structure and mantle dynamics** | Core to understanding volcanism, tectonics, and planetary evolution |
| **Ice-sheet stability and sea-level commitment** | Critical for coastal futures and climate policy |
| **AMOC and circulation tipping risks** | Major implications for regional climate and carbon cycling |
| **Carbon cycle feedbacks and permafrost release** | Strong relevance to near-term climate trajectories |
| **Water availability and groundwater depletion** | Links hydrology, climate, food systems, and infrastructure |
| **Critical minerals and sustainable extraction** | Essential for energy transition and geopolitics |
| **Mars habitability and comparative planetology** | Connects Earth science to planetary evolution and astrobiology |

### Active current directions

- **Extreme-event attribution and regional climate risk**
- **ML for weather forecasting, earthquake detection, and remote sensing**
- **Carbon capture, mineralization, and geologic storage**
- **Critical-zone science and coupled soil-water-rock-biota systems**
- **High-resolution paleoclimate reconstruction**
- **Permafrost, wildfire, and cryosphere change**
- **Ocean deoxygenation and acidification**
- **Planetary mission science for Mars, Europa, Titan, and Venus**

## 5.7 Career Paths

| Path | Typical destinations |
|---|---|
| **Academic research** | Universities, postdoctoral research, observatories, field stations, marine institutes |
| **Government science** | USGS, NOAA, NASA, EPA, DOE labs, geological surveys, meteorological services |
| **Environmental and water consulting** | Site assessment, remediation, groundwater, environmental compliance, hazard analysis |
| **Energy and resource sectors** | Critical minerals, geothermal, subsurface characterization, carbon storage, legacy oil and gas |
| **Climate and weather services** | Forecasting, climate analytics, catastrophe modeling, risk and resilience consulting |
| **GIS / remote sensing / geospatial analytics** | Mapping firms, satellite companies, environmental intelligence, planning |
| **Natural hazards and infrastructure** | Seismic risk, landslides, flooding, coastal change, geotechnical interfaces |
| **Planetary and space science** | Mission teams, data analysis, planetary labs, aerospace Earth-observation roles |

Some tracks also use professional credentials such as **Professional Geologist (PG)** or engineering licensure in applied geotechnical or environmental settings.

## 5.9 What a Full Picture of Earth and Environmental Research Requires

A research-ready Earth science roadmap should make clear:

1. how students move from **introductory geology and Earth-system science** into tectonics, climate, water, geochemistry, or planetary specialization;
2. how the field depends on **field observation, mapping, laboratory analysis, remote sensing, and numerical modeling** together;
3. how graduate training is shaped by **field seasons, instrument facilities, large public datasets, and quantitative inference**;
4. which **textbooks** anchor the major undergraduate and graduate pathways;
5. which **papers and framework works** define tectonics, climate science, paleoclimate, hazards, and Earth-system thinking;
6. and how the field connects to **energy, hazards, policy, environment, and planetary science**.

That is what turns an Earth science summary into a genuine roadmap toward research competence.
