# 35. History

## Degree Programs

| Level | Programs |
|---|---|
| Undergraduate | History (BA), Public History, History of Science, Area Studies-linked history majors |
| Masters | MA in History, MA in Public History, Museum Studies, Archival Studies |
| PhD | History with geographic or thematic specialization |

---

## 35.1 Core Curriculum (Undergraduate)

### Core History Courses

| Course | Content |
|---|---|
| **Historical Methods & Historiography** | Primary vs. secondary sources, archival method, citation, historical argument, major schools of interpretation |
| **World History Survey** | Comparative and connected histories from antiquity to globalization; empires, trade, migration, religion, environment |
| **Regional/National Survey** | Commonly US, European, East Asian, Latin American, African, or Middle Eastern history |
| **Research Seminar** | Topic formation, source collection, historiographic review, seminar paper or thesis chapter |
| **Capstone / Thesis** | Independent research grounded in primary sources and historiographic debate |

### Common Regional/Thematic Electives
- Ancient, medieval, early modern, modern Europe, United States, Atlantic world, Africa, Latin America, East Asia, South Asia, Middle East, gender history, labor history, environmental history, legal history, history of science, memory studies, public/digital history.

---

## 35.2 Graduate Specialization Areas

#### A. Ancient & Medieval History
- Classical Mediterranean, late antiquity, Byzantine, medieval Europe, Islamic worlds, manuscript cultures, archaeology-adjacent training.

#### B. Early Modern & Atlantic History
- Reformations, state formation, colonialism, slavery, print culture, commercial expansion, global exchanges.

#### C. Modern Europe & United States
- Revolutions, industrialization, nationalism, race and citizenship, war, welfare states, Cold War, social movements.

#### D. Global / Transnational History
- Migration, commodities, borderlands, oceanic histories, empires, decolonization, international institutions.

#### E. Social, Cultural & Intellectual History
- Class, gender, labor, media, knowledge systems, emotions, religion, memory, political thought.

#### F. Public, Environmental & Digital History
- Museums and archives, oral history, preservation, climate and landscape history, GIS/text mining, digital collections.

## 35.3 Key Methods & Sources

| Method | Use |
|---|---|
| **Archival Research** | Government records, letters, newspapers, business records, court files, organizational papers |
| **Historiography** | Mapping interpretive debates and schools of scholarship |
| **Paleography / Language Study** | Reading older scripts and nonmodern languages |
| **Oral History** | Interviews, testimony, memory, community archives |
| **Quantitative History** | Demographic, economic, electoral, or cliometric analysis |
| **Spatial / Digital History** | GIS, mapping, network analysis, database curation, OCR/HTR workflows |
| **Material Culture** | Objects, built environments, images, and landscapes as evidence |
| **Comparative / Transnational Analysis** | Following flows across borders rather than staying inside nation-state frames |

## 35.5 Open Problems & Research Frontiers

- **Globalizing historical narrative** - Building syntheses that do not treat Europe or the nation-state as default scales of analysis.
- **Archives, silences, and repair** - Recovering marginalized voices, dealing with absence, and rethinking ownership of historical records.
- **Climate and environmental history** - Integrating climate proxies, landscape change, and environmental justice into mainstream historical explanation.
- **Digital history** - Better OCR/HTR, linked data, and computational analysis without flattening context.
- **Memory politics** - Monuments, reparations, museum interpretation, and contested public narratives.
- **History of capitalism and empire** - Renewed debates on slavery, finance, extraction, and global inequality.
