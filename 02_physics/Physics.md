# 2. Physics

## Degree Programs

| Level | Programs | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Undergraduate | Physics (BA/BS), Applied Physics, Engineering Physics, Astronomy & Physics (combined) | The standard research-university structure; some schools distinguish BA as broader/liberal-arts and BS as more technical |
| Masters | MS in Physics, MS in Applied Physics, MS in Optical Science, MS in Medical Physics | Often used for professional specialization, lab preparation, or PhD preparation |

**Scope note:** A full physics research roadmap must cover theory, experiment, and computation. It should show not only what students learn, but also how they become research-capable physicists.

## 2.1 Core Curriculum (Undergraduate)

Physics undergrad education is remarkably consistent worldwide. The first two years build mathematical fluency and physical intuition; the last two introduce the four pillars of modern theoretical physics plus laboratory and computational practice.

### A. Foundations (Years 1-2)

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **General Physics I (Mechanics)** | Kinematics, Newton's laws, energy, momentum, rotation, oscillations, gravity | The first full model of physical law, problem-solving, and idealization |
| **General Physics II (E&M, Waves, Optics)** | Electrostatics, circuits, magnetism, Faraday's law, Maxwell's equations (intro), geometric and wave optics | Electromagnetism is one of the deepest and most experimentally precise theories in science |
| **Modern Physics** | Special relativity, early quantum ideas, atomic structure, radioactivity, elementary particles (intro) | The bridge from classical intuition to twentieth-century physics |
| **Mathematical Methods for Physics** | Vector calculus, linear algebra, ODEs/PDEs, complex variables, Fourier methods, tensors (intro) | Physicists need math with computational and modeling emphasis |
| **Computational Physics (intro)** | Python/MATLAB/Julia, numerical integration, simulation, data analysis, visualization | Modern physics is inseparable from computation |
| **Introductory Laboratory** | Measurement, uncertainty, basic experiments, plotting, reports | Teaches the culture of evidence, instrumentation, and error analysis |

**Common introductory texts:** Halliday, Resnick & Walker - *Fundamentals of Physics*; Young & Freedman - *University Physics*.

### B. Upper-Division Core - The Four Pillars

These are the universal backbone of the physics major and the main qualifying-exam subjects in most PhD programs.

#### Pillar 1: Classical Mechanics
| Topic | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Lagrangian mechanics | Least action, generalized coordinates, constraints, Euler-Lagrange equations | The conceptual language of modern theoretical physics |
| Hamiltonian mechanics | Phase space, canonical transformations, Poisson brackets | Essential preparation for quantum theory, statistical mechanics, and field theory |
| Rigid body dynamics | Inertia tensor, Euler angles, rotating frames | Standard advanced mechanics toolkit |
| Nonlinear dynamics and chaos | Fixed points, bifurcation, chaos, Lyapunov exponents | Shows where simple laws produce complex behavior |

#### Pillar 2: Electromagnetism
| Topic | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Electrostatics and magnetostatics | Boundary-value problems, multipoles, vector potential | Core mathematical training in fields and potentials |
| Maxwell's equations | Differential and integral forms, waves, radiation | The archetype of a field theory |
| Relativistic electrodynamics | Lorentz covariance, four-vectors, field tensor | Connects E&M to special relativity and modern particle theory |

#### Pillar 3: Quantum Mechanics
| Topic | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Formalism | Hilbert spaces, operators, observables, measurement postulates | The conceptual basis of microscopic physics |
| Exactly solvable systems | Harmonic oscillator, hydrogen atom, barriers, angular momentum | Provides the standard worked models of quantum theory |
| Approximation theory | Perturbation, variational methods, WKB, scattering | The practical toolkit for realistic systems |
| Identical particles and spin | Fermions, bosons, exchange symmetry, spin coupling | Essential for atomic, condensed matter, and particle physics |

#### Pillar 4: Statistical Mechanics & Thermodynamics
| Topic | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Thermodynamics | Laws, entropy, free energies, Maxwell relations | The macroscopic language of equilibrium physics |
| Statistical ensembles | Microcanonical, canonical, grand canonical | Connects microscopic states to macroscopic behavior |
| Quantum statistics | Fermi-Dirac, Bose-Einstein, blackbody radiation | Essential for condensed matter, AMO, and astrophysics |
| Phase transitions and criticality | Ising model, universality, order parameters | A central bridge between many-body theory and experiment |

### C. Additional Upper-Division Courses

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Optics** | Diffraction, interference, polarization, lasers, Fourier optics | Core for AMO, photonics, and instrumentation |
| **Solid State / Condensed Matter (intro)** | Crystals, phonons, band theory, semiconductors | Gateway to the largest modern physics subfield |
| **Nuclear & Particle Physics (intro)** | Nuclear structure, decay, Standard Model overview | Introduces the physics of fundamental matter |
| **General Relativity (intro)** | Curved spacetime, Einstein equations, black holes, cosmology | Key preparation for gravitation and cosmology |
| **Electronics / Experimental Methods** | Circuits, amplifiers, signal processing, DAQ, vacuum basics | Practical lab competence for experimental research |
| **Intermediate / Advanced Lab** | Reproducing classic experiments, spectroscopy, detectors, vacuum, lasers | The point where students begin to resemble junior researchers |

### What an excellent undergraduate should be able to do
By graduation, a future researcher should be able to:
- solve standard problems in the four pillars;
- write clean lab reports and estimate uncertainties responsibly;
- program and visualize physical models;
- read upper-level textbooks independently;
- follow seminar-style expositions and begin reading review articles.

## 2.2 Graduate Depth (MS & PhD)

Graduate physics shifts from mastering canonical theory to joining a research group, learning a specialized literature, and producing original results through theory, experiment, computation, or some combination.

### First-Year Graduate Core

| Course | What becomes deeper | Standard role in PhD training |
|---|---|---|
| **Classical Mechanics** | Hamilton-Jacobi theory, canonical perturbation theory, classical field theory, Noether's theorem | Core theory training for quals and theoretical work |
| **Electrodynamics** | Green's functions, relativistic covariant formulation, radiation, waveguides, advanced boundary problems | Standard first-year pillar course |
| **Quantum Mechanics** | Path integrals, density matrices, symmetries, second quantization, relativistic QM | The gateway to particle, condensed matter, AMO, and quantum information |
| **Statistical Mechanics** | Linear response, renormalization group, phase transitions, nonequilibrium ideas | Central for condensed matter, cosmology, biophysics, and information theory |
| **Mathematical / Computational Methods** | Group theory, asymptotics, differential geometry, numerical methods, Monte Carlo | Often the bridge from coursework to research techniques |

### Graduate milestones that matter in practice

| Milestone | What it usually involves |
|---|---|
| **Qualifying exams** | Written exams in the pillars and sometimes math methods or specialty areas |
| **Research rotations or group matching** | Common in experimental and applied programs; students choose among labs or advisors |
| **Advisor selection** | A decisive step because subfield, publication style, and lab culture vary strongly |
| **Seminars and journal clubs** | The fastest way to learn current problems, methods, and field conventions |
| **Reading frontier literature** | Students often start with review articles and lecture notes before original papers |
| **Proposal / candidacy exam** | Formal shift from coursework to dissertation research |
| **Dissertation** | Original work that may be theoretical, computational, experimental, or instrumentational |

### Major Specialization Areas

#### A. Condensed Matter Physics
The study of phases of matter and emergent collective behavior.

**Key topics**
- band theory, electronic structure, semiconductors
- superconductivity and superfluidity
- magnetism and spin systems
- topological phases and quantum Hall physics
- strongly correlated matter and non-Fermi liquids
- soft matter, polymers, colloids, active matter
- quantum transport and mesoscopic systems

**Typical tools**
- ARPES, STM, neutron and X-ray scattering, transport, cryogenics, DFT, tensor methods

#### B. Particle Physics / High-Energy Physics
The study of fundamental particles, interactions, and quantum field theory.

**Key topics**
- Standard Model and gauge theory
- collider physics and detector analysis
- QCD, electroweak theory, Higgs physics
- flavor, CP violation, neutrino physics
- beyond-the-Standard-Model scenarios
- lattice QCD and nonperturbative field theory

**Typical tools**
- QFT, group theory, Monte Carlo event generators, detector simulation, ROOT, large collaborations

#### C. Astrophysics & Cosmology
The study of stars, galaxies, black holes, cosmology, and observation-driven theory.

**Key topics**
- stellar structure and evolution
- galactic dynamics and the interstellar medium
- galaxy formation and active galactic nuclei
- cosmology, inflation, dark matter, dark energy
- gravitational-wave astronomy and multi-messenger astrophysics
- exoplanets and planetary systems

**Typical tools**
- telescopes, survey pipelines, numerical simulations, Bayesian inference, signal extraction from large datasets

#### D. Nuclear Physics
The physics of nuclei, nuclear matter, and strong-interaction many-body systems.

**Key topics**
- shell model and collective structure
- nuclear reactions and scattering
- heavy-ion collisions and quark-gluon plasma
- nuclear astrophysics
- electroweak probes and neutrino-nucleus interactions

#### E. Atomic, Molecular & Optical Physics (AMO)
Precision physics at the atomic scale and a major center of quantum science.

**Key topics**
- atomic structure and spectroscopy
- laser cooling, trapping, and ultracold gases
- quantum optics, cavity QED, squeezed light
- precision clocks and symmetry tests
- strong-field and attosecond science

#### F. General Relativity & Gravitation
Einstein's geometric theory of gravity and its modern extensions.

**Key topics**
- differential geometry for GR
- black holes, singularities, and horizons
- gravitational waves and numerical relativity
- cosmology and relativistic astrophysics
- quantum gravity approaches and information paradoxes

#### G. Plasma Physics and Fusion
The physics of ionized matter in laboratory, industrial, and astrophysical settings.

**Key topics**
- kinetic theory and Vlasov dynamics
- MHD, waves, and instabilities
- magnetic confinement and inertial confinement fusion
- space and astrophysical plasmas

#### H. Biophysics & Soft Matter
Physics applied to living systems and complex materials.

**Key topics**
- polymer and membrane physics
- molecular motors and nonequilibrium systems
- protein folding and single-molecule experiments
- active matter and biological transport

#### I. Quantum Information & Quantum Computing
An interface field linking physics, computer science, and engineering.

**Key topics**
- qubits, gates, and entanglement
- error correction and fault tolerance
- quantum algorithms and simulation
- hardware platforms: superconducting, ion, photonic, neutral-atom, topological

#### J. String Theory & Mathematical Physics
The most abstract end of theoretical high-energy physics.

**Key topics**
- string worldsheet theory and supersymmetry
- compactification and dualities
- AdS/CFT and holography
- topological field theory and amplitudes

### How physicists actually become researchers
- **Experimental physicists** become research-ready by mastering instrumentation, analysis pipelines, and collaboration routines.
- **Theoretical physicists** usually move from graduate texts to review papers, then to the active literature.
- **Computational physicists** need both domain theory and production-grade numerical workflow skills.
- In all cases, **advisor fit, seminar culture, and repeated exposure to current literature** matter as much as formal coursework.

## 2.3 Key Methods, Tools, and Research Infrastructure

### Theoretical methods

| Method | Typical use |
|---|---|
| **Symmetry and group theory** | Organizing laws, conserved quantities, selection rules, gauge structure |
| **Perturbation theory** | Approximate solutions around solvable models |
| **Variational methods** | Approximations from energy minimization or action principles |
| **Renormalization group** | Connecting physical behavior across scales |
| **Effective field theory** | Low-energy descriptions that retain only relevant degrees of freedom |
| **Path integrals** | Quantum and statistical formulations with strong field-theory applications |
| **Dimensional analysis and scaling** | Fast structural understanding of regimes and dominant effects |
| **Phase-space methods** | Classical mechanics, transport, and dynamical-systems analysis |

### Computational methods

| Method | Typical application |
|---|---|
| **Monte Carlo** | Statistical sampling in many-body systems, lattice models, and Bayesian inference |
| **Molecular dynamics** | Materials, chemical, and biological simulations |
| **Density functional theory** | Electronic structure of materials and molecules |
| **Finite-element / finite-volume methods** | Engineering-scale E&M, fluid, and thermal simulations |
| **N-body simulations** | Cosmology, stellar systems, gravitational dynamics |
| **Lattice gauge theory** | Nonperturbative QFT calculations |
| **Tensor networks** | Low-dimensional quantum many-body systems |
| **Numerical relativity** | Binary merger and strong-gravity simulations |
| **Signal extraction / statistical pipelines** | Astronomy, particle physics, gravitational-wave detection |

### Experimental techniques

| Technique | Typical use |
|---|---|
| **Spectroscopy** | AMO, condensed matter, chemistry-adjacent physics, astrophysics |
| **Scattering** | Structure and interaction studies in condensed matter, nuclear, and particle physics |
| **Microscopy** | Surface and nanoscale imaging in condensed matter and materials physics |
| **Particle detectors** | Collider, nuclear, and astroparticle experiments |
| **Interferometry** | Precision metrology, optics, gravitational-wave physics |
| **Cryogenics** | Superconductivity, quantum devices, ultracold experiments |
| **Vacuum systems and beamlines** | Surface science, accelerators, AMO, synchrotron work |
| **Laser systems** | Spectroscopy, trapping, precision control, ultrafast science |

### Core software and infrastructure

| Tool | Typical use |
|---|---|
| **Python (NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib)** | General computation, simulation, and analysis |
| **Mathematica / Maple** | Symbolic manipulation and analytic work |
| **ROOT** | High-energy and nuclear data analysis |
| **Geant4** | Detector simulation |
| **VASP / Quantum ESPRESSO / Gaussian** | Electronic-structure and quantum-chemistry-adjacent workflows |
| **LAMMPS / GROMACS** | Molecular dynamics |
| **COMSOL** | Multiphysics simulation |
| **QuTiP** | Open quantum systems and quantum optics |
| **LaTeX** | Universal scientific writing standard |

### Research infrastructure
- **arXiv** is the main preprint channel for most of physics.
- **Large facilities** matter unusually strongly: CERN, national labs, observatories, synchrotrons, neutron sources, gravitational-wave observatories, fusion devices.
- **Collaboration scale varies widely:** AMO groups may be small; particle and cosmology collaborations may include hundreds or thousands of researchers.
- **Review articles and lecture notes** are often the real entry point into a new subfield before the primary literature.

## 2.5 Canonical Literature and Research Reading Roadmap

Physics is a paper-centered field. A research-ready reading map should therefore combine foundational papers, canonical method papers, landmark experimental discoveries, and modern review/orientation pieces.

### A. Foundational papers every physicist should know
1. **Albert Einstein (1905)** - *On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies*. The founding paper of special relativity.
2. **Albert Einstein (1905)** - *On a Heuristic Viewpoint Concerning the Production and Transformation of Light*. Foundational quantum paper on the photoelectric effect.
3. **Albert Einstein (1905)** - *On the Movement of Small Particles Suspended in Stationary Liquids Required by the Molecular-Kinetic Theory of Heat*. Foundational Brownian-motion paper.
4. **Niels Bohr (1913)** - *On the Constitution of Atoms and Molecules*. Key step in atomic theory.
5. **Werner Heisenberg (1925)** - *Quantum-Theoretical Re-interpretation of Kinematic and Mechanical Relations*. Foundational matrix mechanics paper.
6. **Erwin Schrödinger (1926)** - *Quantization as an Eigenvalue Problem*. Foundational wave mechanics paper sequence.
7. **Paul Dirac (1928)** - *The Quantum Theory of the Electron*. Relativistic quantum mechanics and prediction of antimatter.
8. **Yang and Mills (1954)** - *Conservation of Isotopic Spin and Isotopic Gauge Invariance*. Foundational non-Abelian gauge theory paper.
9. **Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer (1957)** - *Theory of Superconductivity*. The defining paper of BCS theory.
10. **John Bell (1964)** - *On the Einstein Podolsky Rosen Paradox*. Foundational for entanglement and quantum foundations.

### B. Canonical methods and framework papers
11. **Richard Feynman (1948)** - *Space-Time Approach to Non-Relativistic Quantum Mechanics*. Path-integral formulation.
12. **Metropolis et al. (1953)** - *Equation of State Calculations by Fast Computing Machines*. Canonical Monte Carlo method paper.
13. **Hohenberg and Kohn (1964)** - *Inhomogeneous Electron Gas*. Foundational density-functional theory result.
14. **Kohn and Sham (1965)** - *Self-Consistent Equations Including Exchange and Correlation Effects*. Practical DFT framework.
15. **Kenneth Wilson (1975)** - *The Renormalization Group: Critical Phenomena and the Kondo Problem*. A modern conceptual turning point in many-body and field theory.
16. **Steven White (1992)** - *Density Matrix Formulation for Quantum Renormalization Groups*. Foundational DMRG paper.
17. **Perdew, Burke, and Ernzerhof (1996)** - *Generalized Gradient Approximation Made Simple*. One of the most widely used exchange-correlation approximations in DFT.
18. **Juan Maldacena (1998)** - *The Large N Limit of Superconformal Field Theories and Supergravity*. The foundational AdS/CFT paper.

### C. Landmark subfield-defining results

#### Condensed matter and many-body physics
19. **P. W. Anderson (1958)** - *Absence of Diffusion in Certain Random Lattices*. Foundational localization paper.
20. **R. B. Laughlin (1983)** - *Anomalous Quantum Hall Effect: An Incompressible Quantum Fluid with Fractionally Charged Excitations*. Landmark in topological condensed matter.
21. **Kane and Mele (2005)** - *Quantum Spin Hall Effect in Graphene*. Foundational topological-insulator theory paper.
22. **Novoselov et al. (2004)** - *Electric Field Effect in Atomically Thin Carbon Films*. Landmark graphene paper.

#### Cosmology and astrophysics
23. **Perlmutter et al. (1999)** - *Measurements of $a and $b from 42 High-Redshift Supernovae*. One of the two central acceleration-of-the-universe papers.
24. **Riess et al. (1998)** - *Observational Evidence from Supernovae for an Accelerating Universe and a Cosmological Constant*. The paired discovery paper for dark energy.
25. **Abbott et al. (2016)** - *Observation of Gravitational Waves from a Binary Black Hole Merger*. The GW150914 discovery paper.
26. **Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957)** - *Synthesis of the Elements in Stars*. Foundational nucleosynthesis paper.

#### AMO and quantum information
27. **Aspect, Dalibard, and Roger (1982)** - *Experimental Test of Bell's Inequalities Using Time-Varying Analyzers*. Landmark Bell-test experiment.
28. **Shor (1994)** - *Algorithms for Quantum Computation: Discrete Logarithms and Factoring*. Foundational quantum-computing breakthrough.
29. **Cirac and Zoller (1995)** - *Quantum Computations with Cold Trapped Ions*. Foundational trapped-ion architecture paper.
30. **Wineland et al. (1998)** - *Experimental Issues in Coherent Quantum-State Manipulation of Trapped Atomic Ions*. Canonical early trapped-ion review/architecture piece.

#### Particle, field theory, and gravitation
31. **'t Hooft and Veltman (1972)** - *Regularization and Renormalization of Gauge Fields*. Foundational gauge-theory renormalization paper.
32. **Weinberg (1967)** - *A Model of Leptons*. Electroweak unification cornerstone.
33. **Hawking (1975)** - *Particle Creation by Black Holes*. Foundational black-hole thermodynamics paper.
34. **Bekenstein (1973)** - *Black Holes and Entropy*. The entropy side of black-hole thermodynamics.

### D. Reviews and orientation papers
35. **Philip W. Anderson (1972)** - *More Is Different*. Essential essay on emergence.
36. **Kadanoff (1966)** - *Scaling Laws for Ising Models Near $T_c$*. Early scaling perspective in critical phenomena.
37. **Hasan and Kane (2010)** - *Colloquium: Topological Insulators*. Canonical modern review.
38. **Bloch, Dalibard, and Nascimbène (2012)** - *Quantum Simulations with Ultracold Quantum Gases*. Strong modern AMO orientation review.
39. **Zurek (2003)** - *Decoherence, Einselection, and the Quantum Origins of the Classical*. Standard quantum-foundations review.
40. **Polchinski (2017)** - *The Black Hole Information Problem*. Standard modern high-level review.

### How to read the physics literature
- Start with **standard textbooks**, then **review articles**, then **landmark papers**, then current specialized papers.
- In large fields, read by **subfield cluster**, not chronologically.
- For research readiness, know at least:
  - one standard textbook,
  - one methods paper,
  - one landmark experimental/theoretical paper,
  - and one modern review in your intended area.

## 2.6 Open Problems and Research Frontiers

### Fundamental open questions

| Problem | Area | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Dark matter** | Cosmology / particle physics | One of the central missing ingredients of the standard cosmological picture |
| **Dark energy** | Cosmology | The dominant component of cosmic energy density remains unexplained |
| **Quantum gravity** | Theory | The deepest conflict between general relativity and quantum mechanics |
| **Matter-antimatter asymmetry** | Particle physics / cosmology | Standard Model CP violation appears insufficient |
| **Neutrino masses and ordering** | Particle physics | A clear sign of physics beyond the Standard Model |
| **Black-hole information paradox** | Gravity / QFT / quantum information | A central test of how quantum theory and gravity fit together |
| **High-temperature superconductivity** | Condensed matter | One of the classic unresolved many-body problems |
| **Turbulence** | Fluid dynamics | A flagship unresolved multiscale problem in classical physics |
| **Quantum measurement and foundations** | Foundations | Still unsettled conceptually despite overwhelming empirical success |

### Active frontier clusters
- **Gravitational-wave astronomy** - next-generation detectors, precision source modeling, multi-messenger inference.
- **Quantum materials** - moiré systems, topological phases, spin liquids, non-equilibrium matter.
- **Quantum computing hardware** - scalability, error correction, architecture competition, useful fault tolerance.
- **Fusion energy** - magnetic and inertial confinement, materials, turbulence, reactor engineering.
- **Precision tests of fundamental symmetries** - EDMs, muon anomalies, Lorentz tests, clock comparisons.
- **Machine learning for physics** - fast simulation, inverse problems, symbolic discovery, experimental control.
- **Nonequilibrium quantum dynamics** - thermalization, scrambling, open-system behavior, quantum chaos.
- **Cosmological surveys and inference** - dark-energy constraints, structure growth, early-universe signals.

### Research-frontier judgment
For a future physicist, it is more useful to know **which toolchains and review literatures currently dominate a field** than merely to know the names of famous unsolved problems.

## 2.7 Career Paths

### Academic and research paths
- **Research university faculty** - typically PhD, postdoc sequence, then faculty search.
- **National labs and observatories** - major employers in particle, nuclear, astrophysical, AMO, and applied areas.
- **Teaching-focused academia** - liberal arts colleges and undergraduate institutions.
- **Institute-based research** - Perimeter, KITP, IAS, Kavli institutes, observatory and accelerator centers.

### Industry and applied paths
- **Quantitative finance** - especially for theorists and computational physicists.
- **Data science and machine learning** - statistical modeling, inference, high-dimensional computation.
- **Semiconductors and devices** - condensed matter, optics, materials, nanofabrication.
- **Quantum technology** - hardware, control, error correction, quantum software.
- **Defense and aerospace** - plasma, optics, sensing, simulation, signal processing.
- **Medical physics** - imaging and radiation therapy with additional professional training.
- **Photonics and laser industry** - optics, metrology, ultrafast systems.
- **Energy and fusion** - plasma, materials, modeling, diagnostics.
- **Scientific software and simulation** - HPC, numerical methods, visualization, scientific infrastructure.

### What differs by subfield
- **Experimental physics** often leads naturally to hardware, lab, or instrumentation careers.
- **Theoretical physics** often leads to academia, finance, data science, and highly analytical industry roles.
- **Computational physics** often transitions well into HPC, simulation, ML, and scientific computing roles.

## 2.9 What a Full Picture of Physics Research Requires

A physics file should be considered research-ready only if it gives the learner all of the following:
- a realistic path from undergraduate foundations to graduate specialization;
- a clear view of theory, experiment, and computation as distinct but connected research modes;
- course-by-course textbook guidance by level;
- a literature roadmap mixing classic papers, method papers, landmark discoveries, and modern reviews;
- a concrete sense of how physicists enter research through labs, groups, seminars, and infrastructure;
- a strong map of active frontier questions and cross-disciplinary relevance.

That is the standard this file is intended to meet.
