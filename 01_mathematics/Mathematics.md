# 1. Mathematics

## Degree Programs

| Level | Programs | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Undergraduate | Mathematics (BA/BS), Applied Mathematics, Statistics | The standard research-university structure; some schools also offer Mathematical Sciences or joint Math/CS tracks |
| Adjacent undergraduate variants | Actuarial Science, Financial Mathematics, Data Science (math-heavy) | Useful applied/professional variants, but not substitutes for the core mathematics major |
| Masters | MA/MS in Mathematics, MS in Applied Mathematics, MS in Statistics, MS in Biostatistics, MS in Financial Mathematics | Often used for advanced coursework, industry preparation, or PhD preparation |
| PhD | Pure Mathematics, Applied Mathematics, Statistics & Probability, Mathematical Logic & Foundations | These align with the main graduate research tracks in top universities |

**Scope note:** A strong mathematics research roadmap must cover pure mathematics, applied mathematics, and statistics/probability. It should show not only course topics, but also how students become research-capable mathematicians.

## 1.1 Core Curriculum (Undergraduate)

A strong undergraduate mathematics education usually moves through four stages: computational foundations, proof transition, upper-division core theory, and track-specific depth.

### A. Foundations (Years 1-2)

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Calculus I-III** | Limits, derivatives, integrals, series, multivariable calculus, vector calculus | The standard entry point for nearly every later mathematical and scientific subject |
| **Linear Algebra** | Matrices, vector spaces, eigenvalues, linear maps, inner products | Central to pure math, applied math, statistics, physics, economics, and machine learning |
| **Ordinary Differential Equations** | First- and second-order ODEs, systems, stability, Laplace transforms | The first serious encounter with mathematical modeling of change |
| **Discrete Mathematics** | Logic, sets, counting, graph theory, recurrences, proof techniques | A bridge from calculation to structure and rigorous reasoning |
| **Probability / Intro Statistics** | Random variables, distributions, expectation, estimation, inference | Core preparation for statistics, data science, stochastic processes, and modern applied work |
| **Programming for Mathematical Computing** | Python, MATLAB, Julia, numerical experiments, plotting | Increasingly standard for applied math, statistics, and computational mathematics |

### B. Proof Transition (usually late Year 1 or Year 2)

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Introduction to Proofs / Transition to Advanced Mathematics** | Logic, quantifiers, sets, functions, induction, contradiction, contrapositive, equivalence relations | The single most important transition point between service mathematics and major-level mathematics |

### C. Upper-Division Core - Pure Mathematics Track

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Real Analysis** | Rigorous limits, continuity, differentiation, Riemann integration, sequences/series of functions, metric spaces | The canonical proof-based core course in modern mathematics |
| **Abstract Algebra** | Groups, rings, fields, homomorphisms, quotient structures, Galois theory (intro) | The main structural language of modern pure mathematics |
| **Topology** | Topological spaces, continuity, compactness, connectedness, quotient spaces, fundamental group (intro) | Introduces the global and qualitative viewpoint central to geometry and modern analysis |
| **Complex Analysis** | Analytic functions, contour integration, residues, conformal maps | One of the most elegant and useful subjects in analysis and mathematical physics |
| **Number Theory** | Divisibility, modular arithmetic, primes, quadratic reciprocity, Diophantine equations | A gateway to algebra, cryptography, arithmetic geometry, and analytic number theory |

### D. Upper-Division Core - Applied Mathematics & Statistics Track

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Probability Theory** | Measure-lite or measure-based probability, random variables, LLN, CLT | The main theoretical foundation for statistics, stochastic modeling, and modern data science |
| **Mathematical Statistics** | Estimation, sufficiency, likelihood, testing, asymptotics, Bayesian ideas | Core preparation for graduate statistics and research work |
| **Numerical Analysis** | Error analysis, interpolation, linear systems, numerical differentiation/integration, ODE solvers | Connects mathematics to computation and scientific simulation |
| **Partial Differential Equations** | Heat, wave, Laplace equations, Fourier methods, boundary-value problems | Essential for physics, engineering, fluid dynamics, and modern applied analysis |
| **Optimization** | Linear programming, convexity, duality, nonlinear optimization | Core to operations research, economics, data science, control, and machine learning |
| **Dynamical Systems / Mathematical Modeling** | Stability, phase portraits, bifurcation, model building | Shows how mathematics structures real phenomena in science and engineering |
| **Stochastic Processes** | Markov chains, Poisson processes, Brownian motion, martingales (intro) | The natural next step after probability for finance, operations research, and advanced statistics |

### Typical Advanced Electives
- Differential geometry
- Algebraic topology
- Functional analysis
- Combinatorics / graph theory
- Logic / set theory
- Commutative algebra
- Algebraic geometry (honors or beginning graduate level)
- Numerical linear algebra
- Regression / experimental design / time series

### What an excellent undergraduate should be able to do
By the end of a strong degree, a future researcher should be able to:
- read and write proofs cleanly;
- move comfortably between examples, abstractions, and counterexamples;
- read serious upper-level textbooks independently;
- use computation to test intuition in applied or statistical settings;
- begin reading graduate texts, expository articles, and seminar notes.

## 1.2 Graduate Depth (MS & PhD)

Graduate mathematics shifts from mastering established theory to joining a research community, identifying problems, and producing original work.

### First-Year Graduate Core

| Area | Core topics | Typical role in PhD training |
|---|---|---|
| **Real Analysis / Measure Theory** | Lebesgue measure and integration, $L^p$ spaces, convergence theorems, Radon-Nikodym, product measure | Standard first-year analysis sequence and a foundation for probability, PDE, and functional analysis |
| **Abstract Algebra** | Group theory, ring theory, modules, field extensions, Galois theory, basic category/homological language | Standard first-year algebra sequence and preparation for geometry, number theory, and representation theory |
| **Topology / Geometry** | Point-set topology, manifolds, algebraic topology foundations | Core preparation for geometry, topology, and geometric analysis |
| **Probability / Statistics** | Measure-theoretic probability, asymptotics, inference, stochastic processes | Core in statistics/probability PhD programs and useful in applied math |
| **Applied Analysis / PDE / Numerical Methods** | Sobolev spaces, weak solutions, finite elements, optimization, numerical linear algebra | Standard entry point for applied mathematics research |
| **Logic / Foundations** | Model theory, set theory, computability, proof theory (program-dependent) | Core in logic-oriented graduate training |

### Graduate Milestones That Matter in Practice

| Milestone | What it usually involves |
|---|---|
| **Qualifying exams** | Written exams in core areas such as analysis, algebra, topology, probability, or applied mathematics |
| **Advisor selection** | Choosing a research supervisor whose area, style, and problems fit the student |
| **Reading courses** | Independent or small-group study of advanced books and papers before research begins |
| **Seminars** | The main mechanism for learning active mathematics, current techniques, and open problems |
| **Problem selection** | Moving from broad area knowledge to a tractable, publishable research question |
| **Preprints and journals** | Learning to read arXiv papers, referee-style exposition, and journal-level standards of proof |
| **Teaching and communication** | Teaching, presenting, writing lecture notes, and turning technical work into clear exposition |
| **Dissertation** | A coherent body of original results, usually built from one or more papers or substantial chapters |

### Major Specialization Areas

#### A. Algebra, Number Theory, and Representation Theory
- Group theory, ring theory, module theory, Lie algebras, algebraic number theory, class field theory, automorphic forms, arithmetic geometry, representation theory of finite groups/Lie groups/Lie algebras.
- Research style: deep structural abstraction, heavy use of examples, strong dependence on seminar culture and specialized monographs.

#### B. Algebraic Geometry and Commutative Algebra
- Varieties, schemes, sheaves, cohomology, moduli, singularities, deformation theory, intersection theory, derived methods.
- Research style: long theory chains, advanced prerequisites, strong reliance on reading courses and expository notes.

#### C. Analysis, Functional Analysis, and PDE
- Banach and Hilbert spaces, operator theory, Fourier/harmonic analysis, Sobolev spaces, elliptic/parabolic/hyperbolic PDE, nonlinear PDE, calculus of variations.
- Research style: theorem-proof plus technical estimates; frequent overlap with physics, fluid dynamics, and geometry.

#### D. Geometry and Topology
- Differential geometry, Riemannian geometry, symplectic geometry, algebraic topology, low-dimensional topology, geometric group theory, gauge theory.
- Research style: heavy interaction between formal theory, pictures, and geometric intuition.

#### E. Probability and Statistics
- Stochastic processes, stochastic calculus, large deviations, random matrices, asymptotic statistics, Bayesian methods, nonparametrics, causal inference, high-dimensional statistics.
- Research style: a mix of rigorous theory, asymptotics, computation, and applications.

#### F. Combinatorics and Discrete Mathematics
- Enumerative combinatorics, graph theory, Ramsey theory, probabilistic combinatorics, additive combinatorics, extremal methods, combinatorial optimization.
- Research style: problem-driven, often faster moving, with strong interaction with computer science and probability.

#### G. Logic and Foundations
- Model theory, set theory, computability, descriptive set theory, proof theory, reverse mathematics, category-theoretic and type-theoretic foundations.
- Research style: often conceptually foundational and technically specialized, with strong philosophical and computational connections.

#### H. Applied Mathematics
- Numerical analysis, scientific computing, optimization, control, dynamical systems, fluid dynamics, mathematical biology, inverse problems, uncertainty quantification.
- Research style: theorem + algorithm + simulation, often with interdisciplinary collaborators and domain data.

### How mathematicians actually become researchers
- **Read graduate texts first, not papers first.** Most students enter research through books, lecture notes, and seminar expositions before reading frontier papers.
- **Join seminar ecosystems.** In many areas, the seminar is more important than the formal course once the basics are learned.
- **Learn the literature map.** Knowing which books are standard, which papers changed the field, and which survey articles orient newcomers is a major part of becoming research-ready.
- **Develop taste.** Strong advisors help students learn which problems matter, which tools transfer, and which directions are genuinely fertile.

## 1.3 Key Methods, Tools, and Research Infrastructure

### Core proof and reasoning methods

| Method | Typical use |
|---|---|
| **Direct proof** | Standard derivation from assumptions to conclusion |
| **Contradiction / contrapositive** | Central in analysis, algebra, and number theory |
| **Mathematical induction** | Ubiquitous in discrete math, algebra, and combinatorics |
| **Compactness arguments** | Foundational in topology, analysis, logic, and PDE |
| **Diagonalization** | Core in set theory, logic, computability, and analysis |
| **Probabilistic method** | Canonical in combinatorics and theoretical computer science |
| **Variational methods** | Central in optimization, PDE, geometric analysis, and mechanics |
| **Asymptotic / perturbative reasoning** | Common in applied mathematics, numerical analysis, and dynamical systems |

### Computational and formal tools by branch

| Branch | Common tools |
|---|---|
| **General writing and communication** | LaTeX, BibTeX, Overleaf, Beamer, TikZ |
| **Applied math / numerical analysis** | Python (NumPy/SciPy), MATLAB, Julia, PETSc, FEniCS |
| **Statistics / probability** | R, Python, Stan, JAGS, PyMC |
| **Algebra / number theory / combinatorics** | SageMath, GAP, PARI/GP, Magma, Mathematica |
| **Algebraic geometry / commutative algebra** | Macaulay2, Singular |
| **Optimization / OR** | CVX, CVXPY, Gurobi, MOSEK, JuMP |
| **Formalization / foundations** | Lean, Coq, Isabelle |
| **Visualization / experimentation** | Mathematica, Maple, Python plotting, Jupyter |

### Research infrastructure
- **arXiv** - the main preprint venue for most mathematical research.
- **MathSciNet and zbMATH** - standard databases for verified bibliographic and review information.
- **Seminars, summer schools, and special programs** - often the fastest way to enter a subfield.
- **Graduate texts and lecture notes** - more important for entry into research than journals alone.
- **Collaboration patterns** - range from solo work and small teams in pure mathematics to larger interdisciplinary groups in applied math and statistics.

### Branch-specific research habits
- **Pure mathematics:** long reading periods, seminar-driven learning, slow accumulation of examples and lemmas before original work.
- **Applied mathematics:** theory plus experiments, numerical validation, and frequent use of domain-specific models.
- **Statistics:** theory plus simulation, data analysis, reproducibility, and software implementation.
- **Formal methods / theorem proving:** library building, formalization workflows, and machine-checked proof discipline.

## 1.5 Canonical Literature and Research Reading Roadmap

Mathematics uses a mixed canon: classic papers, foundational monographs, deep graduate texts, and modern breakthrough articles. A strong reading list should therefore include both books and papers.

### A. Core works every mathematician should know
1. **Euclid (c. 300 BCE)** - *Elements*. Historical foundation of axiomatic mathematical exposition.
2. **Georg Cantor (1874)** - *Über eine Eigenschaft des Inbegriffes aller reellen algebraischen Zahlen*. Canonical starting point for set theory and the modern infinite.
3. **David Hilbert (1900)** - *Mathematical Problems*. The most famous research agenda-setting lecture in modern mathematics.
4. **Henri Poincaré (1902)** - *Science and Hypothesis*. Influential on mathematical thought, structure, and scientific reasoning.
5. **George Pólya (1945)** - *How to Solve It*. Classic on mathematical problem solving and heuristic method.
6. **William P. Thurston (1994)** - *On proof and progress in mathematics*. Essential reflection on how mathematical communities actually advance.
7. **Terence Tao (2007)** - *What Is Good Mathematics?* A modern orientation essay on taste, depth, and significance.
8. **Jonathan M. Borwein and David H. Bailey (2003)** - *Mathematics by Experiment*. Important for computational and experimental mathematical practice.

### B. Algebra, number theory, commutative algebra, and algebraic geometry
9. **Emmy Noether (1921)** - *Idealtheorie in Ringbereichen*. Foundational for modern abstract algebra and commutative algebra.
10. **Jean-Pierre Serre (1973)** - *A Course in Arithmetic*. Classic bridge into modular forms, quadratic forms, and arithmetic thinking.
11. **Michael Atiyah and Ian Macdonald (1969)** - *Introduction to Commutative Algebra*. The standard compact entry into modern commutative algebra.
12. **Alexander Grothendieck (1957)** - *Sur quelques points d'algèbre homologique*. The Tohoku paper; foundational for derived functors and modern algebraic geometry.
13. **Robin Hartshorne (1977)** - *Algebraic Geometry*. Canonical graduate text for schemes and sheaf cohomology.
14. **Serge Lang (1994)** - *Algebraic Number Theory*. Classic graduate reference for the algebraic number theory pathway.
15. **Andrew Wiles (1995)** - *Modular elliptic curves and Fermat's Last Theorem*. Landmark modern number theory paper.
16. **Bhargava (2004)** - *Higher Composition Laws I*. Modern example of deep structural number theory.
17. **Peter Scholze (2012)** - *Perfectoid Spaces*. A major modern turning point in arithmetic geometry.

### C. Analysis, functional analysis, harmonic analysis, and PDE
18. **Henri Lebesgue (1902)** - *Intégrale, longueur, aire*. Foundational for modern measure and integration.
19. **Stefan Banach (1932)** - *Théorie des opérations linéaires*. Foundational monograph for functional analysis.
20. **Elias M. Stein (1970)** - *Singular Integrals and Differentiability Properties of Functions*. Canonical harmonic analysis reference.
21. **Lars Hörmander (1963-1985)** - *The Analysis of Linear Partial Differential Operators* (series). Major reference for modern linear PDE and microlocal analysis.
22. **Lawrence C. Evans (1998)** - *Partial Differential Equations*. The standard first graduate PDE text.
23. **John Nash (1958)** - *Continuity of Solutions of Parabolic and Elliptic Equations*. Landmark regularity result.
24. **Luis Caffarelli and Luis Silvestre (2007)** - *An Extension Problem Related to the Fractional Laplacian*. Influential modern PDE paper.
25. **Martin Hairer (2014)** - *A Theory of Regularity Structures*. Major modern breakthrough in stochastic PDE.

### D. Geometry and topology
26. **Michael Atiyah and Isadore Singer (1963)** - *The Index of Elliptic Operators on Compact Manifolds*. A central bridge between analysis, topology, and geometry.
27. **John Milnor (1963)** - *Morse Theory*. Classic text linking topology, geometry, and critical point methods.
28. **Mikhail Gromov (1985)** - *Pseudoholomorphic curves in symplectic manifolds*. Foundational for modern symplectic geometry.
29. **Simon Donaldson (1983)** - *An Application of Gauge Theory to Four-Dimensional Topology*. A landmark 4-manifold paper.
30. **Allen Hatcher (2002)** - *Algebraic Topology*. The standard entry text for the subject.
31. **Grigori Perelman (2002)** - *The Entropy Formula for the Ricci Flow and Its Geometric Applications*. Core paper in the resolution of the Poincaré conjecture.
32. **Grigori Perelman (2003)** - *Ricci Flow with Surgery on Three-Manifolds*. The central surgery paper in the same program.

### E. Probability and statistics
33. **Andrey Kolmogorov (1933)** - *Foundations of the Theory of Probability*. The modern axiomatization of probability.
34. **Joseph L. Doob (1953)** - *Stochastic Processes*. Classic foundation for rigorous stochastic-process theory.
35. **Kiyosi Itô (1944)** - *Stochastic Integral*. Foundational work for stochastic calculus.
36. **R. A. Fisher (1922)** - *On the Mathematical Foundations of Theoretical Statistics*. Foundational modern statistics paper.
37. **Jerzy Neyman and Egon Pearson (1933)** - *On the Problem of the Most Efficient Tests of Statistical Hypotheses*. Canonical hypothesis-testing framework.
38. **Bradley Efron (1979)** - *Bootstrap Methods: Another Look at the Jackknife*. A modern statistical methodology landmark.
39. **Aad van der Vaart (1998)** - *Asymptotic Statistics*. Standard modern asymptotic statistics reference.
40. **Larry Wasserman (2004)** - *All of Statistics*. Widely used compact orientation text for modern statistical thinking.

### F. Combinatorics, discrete mathematics, and theoretical interfaces
41. **Frank P. Ramsey (1930)** - *On a Problem of Formal Logic*. Foundational for Ramsey theory.
42. **Paul Erdős and Alfréd Rényi (1959)** - *On Random Graphs I*. Foundational paper in probabilistic combinatorics.
43. **Richard Stanley (1997, 1999)** - *Enumerative Combinatorics* I-II. Gold-standard reference for modern enumerative combinatorics.
44. **Noga Alon and Joel Spencer (1992; later editions)** - *The Probabilistic Method*. Canonical method text.
45. **Timothy Gowers (2001)** - *A New Proof of Szemerédi's Theorem*. Major modern combinatorics paper.
46. **Ben Green and Terence Tao (2008)** - *The Primes Contain Arbitrarily Long Arithmetic Progressions*. One of the best-known modern additive combinatorics results.
47. **Stephen Cook (1971)** - *The Complexity of Theorem-Proving Procedures*. Essential interface point between mathematics, logic, and theoretical computer science.

### G. Logic and foundations
48. **Kurt Gödel (1931)** - *On Formally Undecidable Propositions of Principia Mathematica and Related Systems I*. The canonical incompleteness paper.
49. **Paul Cohen (1963)** - *The Independence of the Continuum Hypothesis*. Landmark forcing result.
50. **C. C. Chang and H. Jerome Keisler (1973; later editions)** - *Model Theory*. Classic model theory reference.
51. **Kenneth Kunen (1980; later editions)** - *Set Theory*. Standard graduate entry into modern set theory.
52. **Wilfrid Hodges (1993)** - *Model Theory*. Standard advanced model theory text.

### H. Applied mathematics, optimization, and numerical analysis
53. **Courant, Friedrichs, and Lewy (1928)** - *On the Partial Difference Equations of Mathematical Physics*. Foundational CFL stability paper.
54. **Richard Bellman (1957)** - *Dynamic Programming*. Foundational optimization/control monograph.
55. **Rudolf Kalman (1960)** - *A New Approach to Linear Filtering and Prediction Problems*. Foundational for control and estimation.
56. **George Dantzig (1963)** - *Linear Programming and Extensions*. Canonical operations research reference.
57. **Narendra Karmarkar (1984)** - *A New Polynomial-Time Algorithm for Linear Programming*. Landmark optimization paper.
58. **Lloyd N. Trefethen and David Bau III (1997)** - *Numerical Linear Algebra*. Standard modern numerical linear algebra text.
59. **Stephen Boyd and Lieven Vandenberghe (2004)** - *Convex Optimization*. Canonical modern optimization text.
60. **Emmanuel Candès, Justin Romberg, and Terence Tao (2006)** - *Robust Uncertainty Principles: Exact Signal Reconstruction from Highly Incomplete Frequency Information*. Landmark compressed sensing paper.

### How to read this literature
- Start with **core works + graduate texts** before attempting specialized frontier papers.
- Use **subfield clusters** rather than trying to read mathematics linearly.
- In many areas, a **survey or graduate monograph** is the real gateway; the original paper becomes readable only after that preparation.
- For research readiness, aim to know the **standard graduate text, the classic landmark result, and one modern frontier paper** in your intended area.

## 1.6 Open Problems and Research Frontiers

### Major classical open problems
- **Riemann Hypothesis** - central to analytic number theory and the distribution of primes.
- **P vs NP** - the flagship problem at the logic/CS boundary.
- **Navier-Stokes existence and smoothness** - a core PDE and fluid-dynamics problem.
- **Yang-Mills existence and mass gap** - central to mathematical physics.
- **Hodge conjecture** - major problem in algebraic geometry.
- **Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture** - a central arithmetic geometry problem.

### Major active frontier clusters

| Frontier | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Arithmetic geometry and the Langlands program** | Continues to unify number theory, representation theory, and geometry |
| **Perfectoid methods and $p$-adic geometry** | Major modern toolkit reshaping arithmetic geometry |
| **Geometric analysis and Ricci / mean-curvature style flows** | Strong interaction between PDE, geometry, and topology |
| **Stochastic PDE and rough structures** | Important in probability, mathematical physics, and scaling limits |
| **Random matrices and high-dimensional probability** | Links probability, statistics, number theory, and data science |
| **Optimization and learning theory** | Deep overlap with statistics, operations research, and machine learning |
| **Formalized mathematics and proof assistants** | Growing importance of Lean, Coq, and machine-checked mathematics |
| **Topological data analysis and applied topology** | Shows how abstract invariants can enter modern data analysis |
| **Combinatorics and additive methods** | Continues to influence number theory, CS theory, and probability |
| **Fluid dynamics and turbulence** | A central unresolved applied-mathematics frontier |

### Research-frontier judgment
For a future researcher, it is not enough to know famous unsolved problems. More important is learning:
- which tools are currently powerful in a field,
- which expository works explain those tools,
- which problems are tractable for a first paper,
- and which frontier areas connect strongly to neighboring disciplines.

## 1.7 Career Paths

### Academic and research paths
- **PhD -> postdoc -> faculty** - the classic pure-math route; highly competitive.
- **Research institutes** - IAS, MSRI/SLMath, IHES, Fields Institute, Oberwolfach, Simons-related programs.
- **Teaching-focused academia** - liberal arts colleges, teaching universities, community colleges.

### Industry and applied paths
- **Quantitative finance** - probability, stochastic processes, optimization, numerical PDE.
- **Data science / machine learning** - statistics, linear algebra, optimization, high-dimensional probability.
- **Operations research and logistics** - optimization, stochastic modeling, decision science.
- **Cryptography / security** - algebra, number theory, coding theory, discrete mathematics.
- **Scientific computing / engineering** - PDE, numerical analysis, simulation, inverse problems.
- **Biostatistics / public health / pharma** - mathematical statistics, causal inference, survival analysis, clinical trial methods.
- **Government and national labs** - cryptography, modeling, scientific computing, defense analysis, forecasting.

### What differs by branch
- **Pure mathematics** careers are more concentrated in academia and research institutes.
- **Applied mathematics** has the broadest mobility into engineering, tech, and industry research.
- **Statistics / probability** usually offers the widest range of nonacademic careers.

## 1.9 What a Full Picture of Mathematics Research Requires

A mathematics file should be considered research-ready only if it gives the learner all of the following:
- a realistic curriculum from first courses to graduate specialization;
- a clear map of pure, applied, and statistical branches;
- course-by-course textbook recommendations by level;
- a literature roadmap that mixes foundational books, landmark papers, and modern frontier work;
- an explanation of how mathematicians actually enter research through seminars, reading courses, advisors, and preprints;
- a clear sense of open problems, active tools, and interdisciplinary relevance.

That is the standard this file is intended to meet.
