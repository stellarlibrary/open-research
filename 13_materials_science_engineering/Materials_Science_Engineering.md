# 13. Materials Science & Engineering

## Degree Programs

| Level | Common programs | What changes by level |
|---|---|---|
| Undergraduate | BS in Materials Science and Engineering, Materials Engineering, Metallurgical Engineering, sometimes tracks in polymers, electronic materials, or biomaterials | Builds the core structure-processing-properties-performance framework of the field |
| Master's | MS/MEng in Materials Science and Engineering, Metallurgy, Polymer Science, Ceramics, Electronic Materials, Biomaterials, or Computational Materials | Adds specialization, deeper characterization, and stronger design or research focus |
| Doctoral | PhD in Materials Science and Engineering or affiliated fields such as metallurgy, polymer science, ceramics, nanomaterials, electronic materials, or materials chemistry | Centers on original research using synthesis, characterization, modeling, and theory |
| Cross-disciplinary variants | Joint programs with chemistry, physics, mechanical engineering, chemical engineering, biomedical engineering, or electrical engineering | Reflects the field's role as a bridge between fundamental matter science and engineered performance |

Materials science and engineering asks how **structure at multiple scales** controls properties and how processing can be used to design matter for mechanical, electrical, optical, chemical, biological, and energy applications.

---

## 13.1 Core Curriculum (Undergraduate)

Strong MSE programs revolve around the field's central logic: **processing -> structure -> properties -> performance**. Students usually take a chemistry- and physics-heavier version of engineering science than many other majors.

### Foundation sequence

| Course | Typical content | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Calculus, differential equations, linear algebra** | Modeling, continuum reasoning, transport, numerical methods | Needed for kinetics, mechanics, and computational materials work |
| **Physics and chemistry** | Mechanics, electromagnetism, thermodynamics, quantum ideas, general and physical chemistry | Supplies the atomic, electronic, and thermodynamic basis of materials behavior |
| **Programming and data analysis** | Scientific computing, statistics, visualization | Increasingly important for simulation, microscopy/image analysis, and materials informatics |

### Core upper-division MSE areas

| Area | Typical courses | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Structure of materials** | Crystallography, defects, diffraction, amorphous structure, polymers | Structure is the organizing language of the field |
| **Thermodynamics of materials** | Free energy, phase equilibria, binary/ternary diagrams, solution behavior | Governs stability, transformations, and processing windows |
| **Kinetics and phase transformations** | Diffusion, nucleation, growth, precipitation, recrystallization, solidification | Explains how real microstructures form over time |
| **Mechanical behavior** | Elasticity, plasticity, dislocations, fracture, fatigue, creep | Core for structural materials and reliability |
| **Electronic, magnetic, and optical properties** | Band theory, semiconductors, dielectrics, magnetism, photonics | Required for functional and device materials |
| **Polymers** | Synthesis, chain statistics, viscoelasticity, thermal transitions, processing | Major materials class with its own physics and manufacturing logic |
| **Ceramics and glasses** | Crystal chemistry, sintering, brittle behavior, functional ceramics | Central to high-temperature, structural, and electroceramic systems |
| **Characterization methods** | XRD, SEM, TEM, spectroscopy, thermal analysis, mechanical testing | Materials research depends on seeing structure and defects directly |
| **Materials selection and design** | Ashby methods, property maps, sustainability, manufacturing constraints | Connects science to engineering decisions |

### Typical laboratory and practical experiences

- **Metallography and microstructure labs**: polishing, etching, microscopy, grain-size and phase analysis
- **Diffraction and spectroscopy labs**: XRD, FTIR/Raman, XPS, elemental analysis
- **Thermal and mechanical testing**: DSC, TGA, tensile tests, hardness, creep, fracture, fatigue
- **Processing labs**: casting, heat treatment, sintering, polymer processing, thin-film deposition, additive manufacturing
- **Materials-selection projects** using property databases and design constraints

## 13.2 Graduate Depth (MS & PhD)

Graduate materials training moves from learning known material classes to discovering or engineering new ones. The field combines theory, synthesis, processing, microscopy, spectroscopy, and simulation more tightly than most disciplines.

### Typical first-year graduate focus

| Area | Typical graduate emphasis | Common milestones |
|---|---|---|
| **Structural materials / metallurgy** | Phase transformations, dislocation theory, alloy design, fracture/fatigue, processing | Qualifiers, microscopy or mechanical-testing platform training |
| **Ceramics and electroceramics** | Defect chemistry, sintering, ionic transport, high-temperature behavior | Synthesis plus characterization depth |
| **Polymers and soft materials** | Polymer physics, rheology, self-assembly, interfaces, processing | Lab-intensive synthesis or soft-matter modeling |
| **Electronic, magnetic, and photonic materials** | Solid-state physics, thin films, semiconductors, quantum/optical materials | Strong theory plus fabrication/characterization integration |
| **Biomaterials and biointerfaces** | Host response, degradable systems, bioactive surfaces, tissue interaction | Interdisciplinary work bridging biology and materials |
| **Computational materials science** | DFT, molecular dynamics, phase-field, CALPHAD, materials informatics | Strong computing, data, and theory-heavy qualifying path |

### Typical PhD structure

- **Advanced coursework** in thermodynamics, kinetics, defects, electronic structure, characterization, and the chosen materials class
- **Advisor selection and facility training** in synthesis, microscopy, spectroscopy, cleanroom work, or HPC workflows
- **Qualifying exams or candidacy milestones** linking fundamentals to specialization
- **Seminars and journal clubs** that emphasize structure-property reasoning and critical reading of data-rich papers
- **Dissertation research** that often integrates processing, characterization, and modeling rather than using only one method family

### Major research tracks

#### Structural materials and metallurgy
- Steels, superalloys, high-entropy alloys, lightweight alloys, deformation mechanisms, fatigue, creep, corrosion, welding, additive-manufacturing metallurgy

#### Ceramics, glasses, and inorganic materials
- Structural ceramics, glass science, cementitious materials, solid electrolytes, electroceramics, refractories, ceramic matrix composites

#### Polymers and soft matter
- Polymer synthesis, self-assembly, rheology, membranes, hydrogels, elastomers, sustainable plastics, responsive materials

#### Electronic, photonic, and magnetic materials
- Semiconductors, wide-bandgap materials, thin films, quantum materials, ferroelectrics, spintronics, photovoltaics, display and sensor materials

#### Nanomaterials and surfaces
- Nanoparticles, nanotubes, graphene and 2D systems, catalysis, coatings, surface modification, interface science

#### Biomaterials and biointerfaces
- Implant materials, tissue-engineering scaffolds, drug-delivery materials, degradable polymers, antimicrobial and immune-modulating surfaces

#### Computational materials science and informatics
- First-principles calculations, molecular simulation, phase-field modeling, CALPHAD, high-throughput discovery, AI-guided materials design

## 13.3 Key Methods, Tools, and Research Infrastructure

### Core characterization and analytical tools

| Tool family | Examples | Common role |
|---|---|---|
| **Diffraction and scattering** | XRD, SAXS/WAXS, neutron scattering | Phase identification, crystal structure, texture, nanoscale organization |
| **Electron microscopy** | SEM, TEM, STEM, EBSD, EDS, EELS | Microstructure, defects, interfaces, composition, nanoscale imaging |
| **Surface and chemical analysis** | XPS, AES, SIMS, FTIR, Raman | Surface chemistry, bonding, contamination, functional groups |
| **Thermal analysis** | DSC, TGA, DMA, dilatometry | Phase transitions, decomposition, viscoelasticity, thermal stability |
| **Mechanical testing** | Tensile, compression, nanoindentation, fracture, fatigue, creep | Structure-property-performance validation |
| **Electrical, magnetic, and optical testing** | Hall measurements, impedance, magnetometry, spectroscopy | Functional materials evaluation |

### Computational and design tools

| Tool family | Examples | Common role |
|---|---|---|
| **First-principles and atomistic simulation** | VASP, Quantum ESPRESSO, ABINIT, LAMMPS, GROMACS | Electronic structure, defect energetics, molecular behavior |
| **Thermodynamics and kinetics tools** | Thermo-Calc, DICTRA, CALPHAD workflows, phase-field codes | Phase stability, diffusion, solidification, process design |
| **Continuum and multiphysics tools** | Abaqus, COMSOL, ANSYS | Coupled mechanical, thermal, electrical, or transport problems |
| **Materials informatics platforms** | Python, ML libraries, Materials Project, pymatgen, matminer | Data-driven discovery and screening |
| **Materials-selection tools** | Granta EduPack, Ashby charts, property databases | Application-driven design and tradeoffs |

### Experimental infrastructure

- **Synthesis platforms**: furnaces, melt processing, solution synthesis, polymerization, ALD/CVD/PVD, additive-manufacturing systems
- **Cleanroom and thin-film facilities** for electronic and photonic materials
- **Microscopy centers and shared characterization facilities** central to modern materials research
- **Synchrotron, neutron, and national-lab resources** for advanced structure determination and operando studies

## 13.4 Foundational Texts and Learning Resources

Materials science needs level-aware textbook guidance because introductory survey books, class-specific texts, microscopy references, and advanced theory books each serve different roles.

| Course / stage | Introductory or accessible | Standard major text | Advanced / graduate continuation | Why this set matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Introduction to MSE** | Shackelford, *Introduction to Materials Science for Engineers* | Callister and Rethwisch, *Materials Science and Engineering: An Introduction* | Askeland and Wright, *The Science and Engineering of Materials* | Standard progression into the field's structure-property framework |
| **Crystallography / structure of materials** | Barrett and Massalski-friendly structure notes or introductory crystallography texts | Cullity and Stock, *Elements of X-Ray Diffraction* | Kelly and Knowles, *Crystallography and Crystal Defects* | Best route from structure basics to diffraction and defect reasoning |
| **Thermodynamics of materials** | DeHoff, *Thermodynamics in Materials Science* | DeHoff, *Thermodynamics in Materials Science* | Porter, Easterling, and Sherif, *Phase Transformations in Metals and Alloys*; Gaskell and Laughlin, *Introduction to the Thermodynamics of Materials* | Core route into stability and phase-equilibria reasoning |
| **Kinetics / phase transformations** | Porter, Easterling, and Sherif, *Phase Transformations in Metals and Alloys* | Porter, Easterling, and Sherif, *Phase Transformations in Metals and Alloys* | Christian, *The Theory of Transformations in Metals and Alloys* | Standard path from undergraduate transformations to graduate depth |
| **Mechanical behavior** | Courtney, *Mechanical Behavior of Materials* | Dieter, *Mechanical Metallurgy* | Hertzberg, Vinci, and Hertzberg, *Deformation and Fracture Mechanics of Engineering Materials*; Hirth and Lothe, *Theory of Dislocations* | Connects experiments to dislocation and fracture theory |
| **Ceramics** | Barsoum, *Fundamentals of Ceramics* | Kingery, Bowen, and Uhlmann, *Introduction to Ceramics* | Chiang, Birnie, and Kingery, *Physical Ceramics* | Best route into structural and functional ceramic behavior |
| **Polymers** | Young and Lovell, *Introduction to Polymers* | Young and Lovell, *Introduction to Polymers* | Rubinstein and Colby, *Polymer Physics*; Odian, *Principles of Polymerization* | Standard progression from broad polymer survey to graduate physics/chemistry |
| **Electronic materials / semiconductors** | Kasap, *Principles of Electronic Materials and Devices* | Kasap, *Principles of Electronic Materials and Devices* | Kittel, *Introduction to Solid State Physics*; Sze and Ng, *Physics of Semiconductor Devices* | Connects device-oriented materials to solid-state foundations |
| **Characterization methods** | Williams and Carter, *Transmission Electron Microscopy* selected chapters | Cullity and Stock, *Elements of X-Ray Diffraction* plus microscopy course notes | Goldstein et al., *Scanning Electron Microscopy and X-Ray Microanalysis*; Williams and Carter, *TEM* | Characterization is central enough that students need modality-specific references |
| **Materials selection and design** | Ashby, *Materials Selection in Mechanical Design* | Ashby, *Materials Selection in Mechanical Design* | Ashby and Jones-style design references; Granta databases | Canonical application-oriented design framework |
| **Computational materials** | LeSar, *Introduction to Computational Materials Science* | LeSar, *Introduction to Computational Materials Science* | Martin, *Electronic Structure*; Frenkel and Smit, *Understanding Molecular Simulation* | Good bridge into modern theory and simulation |

### Additional learning resources

- **Materials Project, AFLOW, OQMD, and NIST databases** for computational and experimental reference data
- **Open microscopy and diffraction software** for analysis practice
- **National-lab user facilities** for synchrotron, neutron, and advanced microscopy exposure
- **MRS, TMS, ACS, APS, ECS, and ACerS communities** depending on specialization

## 13.5 Canonical Literature and Research Reading Roadmap

Materials science is a mixed field. Its core literature includes classic theory papers on defects, diffusion, transformations, and fracture, plus modern breakthroughs in batteries, nanomaterials, electronic materials, and data-driven discovery.

### A. Foundations of defects, deformation, and fracture

1. **Bragg, W. H., and Bragg, W. L. (1913), X-ray diffraction papers.** Foundational structure-determination milestone.
2. **Griffith, A. A. (1921), "The Phenomena of Rupture and Flow in Solids," *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society A*.** Classical fracture-mechanics foundation.
3. **Orowan, E. (1934), Taylor, G. I. (1934), and Polanyi, M. (1934), dislocation-plasticity papers.** Core theory of crystalline plastic deformation.
4. **Frank, F. C., and Read, W. T. (1950), dislocation-source paper in *Physical Review*.** Landmark for dislocation multiplication.
5. **Hall, E. O. (1951) and Petch, N. J. (1953), grain-size strengthening papers.** Canonical Hall-Petch relation.
6. **Eshelby, J. D. (1957), inclusion-elasticity papers.** Foundational micromechanics framework.
7. **Rice, J. R., and Thomson, R. (1974), crack-tip ductile-versus-brittle framework.** Important fracture-mechanics development.

### B. Thermodynamics, diffusion, and phase transformations

8. **Kolmogorov (1937), Johnson and Mehl (1939), and Avrami (1939–1941), transformation-kinetics papers.** Canonical KJMA framework.
9. **Cahn, J. W., and Hilliard, J. E. (1958), "Free Energy of a Nonuniform System," *Journal of Chemical Physics*.** Foundational spinodal-decomposition theory.
10. **Zener, C., grain-growth and pinning papers.** Central microstructural evolution framework.
11. **Porter, Easterling, and Sherif, *Phase Transformations in Metals and Alloys*.** Core orientation reference for transformation-based metallurgy.

### C. Characterization and methods milestones

12. **Binnig, G., Quate, C. F., and Gerber, C. (1986), AFM paper in *Physical Review Letters*.** Major surface-characterization breakthrough.
13. **Blöchl, P. E. (1994), PAW method paper in *Physical Review B*.** Important electronic-structure method.
14. **Kresse, G., and Furthmüller, J. (1996), efficient DFT-iteration papers in *Physical Review B*.** Canonical computational materials reference.
15. **Perdew, J. P., Burke, K., and Ernzerhof, M. (1996), PBE functional paper in *Physical Review Letters*.** Widely used first-principles framework.

### D. Electronic, nanoscale, and energy materials

16. **Whittingham, M. S. (1976), lithium-intercalation battery papers.** Foundational energy-storage milestone.
17. **Goodenough, J. B. (1980), layered oxide cathode chemistry papers.** Crucial Li-ion materials framework.
18. **Yoshino, A. (1985), early rechargeable lithium-ion battery work.** Important practical battery milestone.
19. **Bednorz, J. G., and Müller, K. A. (1986), high-temperature superconductivity paper in *Zeitschrift für Physik B*.** Landmark electronic-materials discovery.
20. **Iijima, S. (1991), carbon-nanotube paper in *Nature*.** Major nanomaterials milestone.
21. **Novoselov, K. S. et al. (2004), graphene-isolation paper in *Science*.** Landmark 2D-materials result.
22. **Kojima, A. et al. (2009), perovskite-solar-absorber paper in *Journal of the American Chemical Society*.** Important modern photovoltaic milestone.
23. **Naguib, M. et al. (2011), MXene-discovery paper in *Advanced Materials*.** Key emerging 2D-material family.
24. **Lee, M. M. et al. (2012) and Kim, H.-S. et al. (2012), efficient solid-state perovskite-solar-cell papers.** Major modern optoelectronic-materials breakthrough.

### E. Structural materials, manufacturing, and design

25. **Ashby, M. F., deformation-mechanism-map and materials-selection works.** Canonical design framework.
26. **Olson, G. B. (1997), computational design of hierarchically structured materials in *Science*.** Important ICME-style vision paper.
27. **Pollock, T. M., and Tin, S. (2006), nickel-based superalloys review in *Journal of Propulsion and Power*.** Core aerospace structural-materials review.
28. **Ritchie, R. O. (2011), strength-toughness tradeoff review in *Nature Materials*.** Excellent modern structural-materials synthesis.
29. **Miracle, D. B., and Senkov, O. N. (2017), high-entropy-alloy review in *Acta Materialia*.** Major emerging-alloys orientation.
30. **DebRoy, T. et al. (2018), additive-manufacturing process-structure-property review in *Progress in Materials Science*.** Key modern manufacturing-literature anchor.

### F. Computational materials and informatics

31. **Jain, A. et al. (2013), Materials Project paper in *APL Materials*.** Landmark data infrastructure for materials discovery.
32. **Butler, K. T. et al. (2018), machine learning for molecular and materials science review in *Nature*.** Important entry to materials informatics.
33. **Recent reviews on autonomous experimentation and inverse materials design.** Essential frontier orientation.

## 13.6 Open Problems and Research Frontiers

| Frontier | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Materials-by-design and inverse discovery** | A central long-term goal is to predict composition and processing for target properties rather than relying on slow trial-and-error |
| **Energy-storage and energy-conversion materials** | Batteries, electrolyzers, fuel cells, and photovoltaics remain materials-limited |
| **Sustainable and circular materials** | Recycling, low-carbon processing, biodegradable polymers, and critical-mineral substitution are major societal priorities |
| **Additive-manufacturing-specific materials** | New process windows create nonequilibrium microstructures that need new design rules and qualification methods |
| **Quantum, electronic, and 2D materials** | Important for next-generation computing, sensing, and communication technologies |
| **High-temperature and extreme-environment materials** | Aerospace, nuclear, and energy systems need materials with better damage tolerance and stability |
| **Bioactive and adaptive biomaterials** | High-impact problems in implants, regeneration, drug delivery, and immune interaction |
| **Autonomous materials laboratories** | Combines robotics, characterization, ML, and closed-loop experimentation for faster discovery |

### Active current directions

- **Solid-state battery electrolytes and interfaces**
- **High-entropy and compositionally complex alloys**
- **Perovskite and tandem photovoltaics**
- **Sustainable polymers and chemical recycling**
- **Operando microscopy and spectroscopy**
- **Foundation-model and graph-learning approaches for materials design**
- **Materials for quantum information and neuromorphic hardware**

## 13.9 What a Full Picture of Materials Science and Engineering Research Requires

A research-ready materials science roadmap should make clear:

1. how students move from **structure, thermodynamics, kinetics, and mechanics** into polymers, ceramics, electronic materials, and characterization;
2. how graduate work branches into **structural, functional, biomaterial, nanoscale, and computational tracks** with very different research cultures;
3. which **methods and infrastructures** matter most, especially microscopy, diffraction, spectroscopy, thermal/mechanical testing, synthesis, and simulation;
4. which **textbooks** anchor each major course from introductory MSE through phase transformations, polymers, ceramics, and solid-state materials;
5. which **papers and framework works** define dislocations, fracture, phase transformations, batteries, nanomaterials, additive manufacturing, and data-driven materials discovery;
6. and how the field links **processing to performance** across energy, electronics, manufacturing, medicine, and sustainability.

That is what turns materials science and engineering from a catalog of material classes into a real roadmap toward research capability.
